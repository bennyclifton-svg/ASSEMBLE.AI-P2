# Client-Side PM Proposal Patterns

Synthesis of strategic thinking, processes, and concepts distilled from three
client-side project management bid documents for distinct project archetypes:

- Fast-track public-domain / retail-edge upgrade on an operating precinct.
- CBD heritage adaptive-reuse mixed-use tower with co-developer / shared
  stratum (one co-developer also acting as D&C contractor).
- Small-scale public-sector health facility on an acquired site under a formal
  multi-stage government program (functional brief → project brief → DA →
  GC21).

The intent is to capture the underlying *bidding logic* — how a competent
client-side PM frames understanding, risk, procurement, resourcing, fees, and
commercial position — independent of any specific project. These patterns are
generalisable to any client-side PM commission proposal.

---

## 1. The Implicit Logic of a Strong PM Proposal

Every section of a strong proposal answers one of five questions for the
client:

1. **Do you understand what I'm actually trying to do, and the constraints
   I'm operating under?** (Project understanding, governance context.)
2. **What is the procurement and contracting *strategy* — and why is it
   right for the market and risk position right now?** (Procurement
   strategy.)
3. **How will you organise the work, the consultants, and the contractor to
   protect *my* interests across time, cost, scope, quality, and risk?**
   (Methodology, interface management, registers, cost/time/scope/quality
   sections.)
4. **Who is doing the work, can they actually deliver it, and do they have
   capacity now?** (Key personnel, current commitments, redundancy.)
5. **What will it cost, on what basis, and how is risk allocated between us
   commercially?** (Fee schedule, hourly rates, terms.)

A weak proposal merely describes services. A strong proposal *makes
arguments* — every methodology choice is justified by reference to project
risk, market conditions, or client objectives.

## 2. Project Understanding — Showing You Did the Pre-Work

The opening section is not a summary; it's evidence of comprehension.
Pattern elements:

- **Cite the documents reviewed**: Statement of Environmental Effects,
  applicant's construction management plan, draft DA submissions, design
  programme, development agreement, partition agreement, clinical service
  plans, models of care. Naming documents shows you've read them.
- **Restate the scope in your own words and identify staging logic** —
  particularly the staging *derived from* other parties' documents (e.g.
  contractor's CMP, consultants' design programme). This signals you
  understand both the formal scope and the operational logic.
- **Call out which stage / element is the *most complex* and why**.
  Typically driven by long-lead procurement, shop-drawing risk,
  deconstruction/reconstruction interfaces, or critical-path interfaces
  with operating tenants / live environments.
- **Mark scope explicitly excluded** with a reason (e.g. excluded because
  not in the time frame for the trading deadline). Excluding scope is a
  positive signal of discipline, not a weakness — provided the boundary
  is rational.
- **Translate the brief dates against your own program**. If you can shave
  months by realistic consent assumptions or concurrent activities, show
  the comparison row-by-row. This is a credibility moment.

## 3. Procurement Strategy — The Beating Heart of the Bid

Procurement strategy is treated as the *strategic core* of a PM proposal,
not a procedural appendix. Recurring strategic moves:

### 3.1 Match contracting form to market conditions and design completeness

- In a busy contractor market, removing trade-pricing risk from the
  contractor makes the package attractive. Construction Management with
  open-book trade pricing (Principal carries trade-pricing risk until
  trades are let as sub-contracts) is a tool to attract competitive
  bidders when the design is high-resolution but tender risk is otherwise
  unappealing.
- Where the project supports a fuller risk transfer (well-resolved
  design, less complex interface), GC21-style or D&C arrangements with
  full design-finalisation risk on the contractor become viable.
- The **fee structure** of a CM contract (lump-sum preliminaries,
  overheads and margin against a PPR with a forecast consent
  responsibility matrix) is itself the procurement lever. The PM's role
  is to make the lump-sum component clean so trade preliminaries are not
  duplicated.

### 3.2 The Principal's Project Requirements (PPR) is the procurement spine

The PPR is the central document; design drawings sit beneath it. PPR
contents that recur:

- Forecast consent matrix (responsibilities, conditions anticipated).
- Order of works / staging plan accounting for tenant negotiations and
  authority-imposed shutdown windows.
- Site management protocol, materials handling, access constraints.
- Hand-over and completion gateways.
- Quality, WHS, and IR expectations.

A clean separation of *design documents* (by Others), *contract documents*
(by Others / legal counsel), and *PPR* (by PM) is the operational
discipline.

### 3.3 The "forecast consent matrix" device

When the authority consent is *not* in hand at tender time, prepare a
*forecast consent matrix* — your best read of likely conditions and where
responsibility for each will sit. Use this as the tender basis, and use
the post-consent variance from it as the trigger for transparent
commercial negotiation. This is a recurring tool for handling tender-
during-DA risk.

### 3.4 Procure from a curated shortlist, not the open market

Shortlist of 3–4 tenderers is the default. The shortlist is justified by
capability, capacity, and the specific risk profile of the project.

### 3.5 Reserve an alternate procurement route

Where one party is positioned as the natural builder (co-developer who is
also a D&C contractor; an incumbent on adjacent works), prepare in
parallel for an **"Other Contractor" route**:

- Document the design and PPR to a standard that *enables* a competitive
  tender if the natural builder's price is rejected.
- Surface the interface and access penalties of switching builders so the
  client understands the trade-off transparently.
- Manage two-contractor-on-one-site logistics (crane locations, materials
  handling, sequencing) as the dominant cost of switching.

### 3.6 Trade-procurement choreography on critical-path items

For complex packages (structural steel, glazing, facade), recognise that
*shop drawing → fabrication → logistics → installation* drives the
critical path. The PM's role is to ensure the contractor's trade
procurement, shop-drawing reviews, and trade tender returns are reviewed
in parallel with the cost manager / QS.

## 4. Design Basis & Design Risk Allocation

Design-risk thinking that recurs:

- **Don't transfer more design risk than the market will price**. A full
  E&O design-risk transfer (D&C with design novation) is unlikely to
  yield competitive pricing when the design is complex (heritage
  envelopes, intricate facade, services interfaces). Better to invest
  Principal-side time advancing the design to high resolution and accept
  a narrower transfer.
- **Identify the missing consultants on the Principal's side**: e.g.
  structural engineer, facade consultant, hydraulic engineer for awning
  drainage, flood consultant for basement design. Don't assume the
  existing team is complete.
- **Permit the contractor to directly procure the late-stage design
  consultants** (where appropriate) to encourage collaborative behaviour
  through post-contract design — but only where risk allocation justifies
  it.
- **Anticipate future stages in current design**: even if subsequent
  stages are deferred, ensure services routing and ground-plane decisions
  in the current stage do not lock out future stages or force expensive
  rework.

## 5. Methodology — Phase by Phase

A robust methodology follows the lifecycle and adds *value commentary* at
each phase:

| Phase | Core activity | Recurring strategic moves |
|---|---|---|
| **Project initiation** | Kick-start meeting, Service Delivery Plan (SDP) draft, governance mobilisation | Procure remaining core consultants; secure single-point-of-contact briefings on prior agreements / co-developer terms before drafting your own SDP. |
| **Feasibility / functional briefing** | Clinical or commercial brief, schedule of accommodation, concept design, project brief for sponsor approval | Delay endorsement of functional brief / SoA until concept design, preliminary cost plan, and *authority pre-application input* have been folded in. Endorsing the brief in a vacuum undermines later phases. |
| **Concept design** | Investigations, ground-plane / basement studies, flood / contamination resolution, options, concept cost plan, value management | Flag in advance the *milestone where executive contingency may be called* — don't ambush the sponsor late. |
| **Schematic / DA** | Pre-application authority consultation #1 and #2, SEE, technical reports, DA lodgement, conditions tracking | Two pre-application meetings with the authority is the recurring pattern — one in concept, one in schematic. Plan for clause-4.6 / merit-assessment pathways early rather than relying on design-competition concessions. |
| **Design development** | DD prior to consent assumed but moderated so re-work is limited if the authority redirects | Start DD only after a view is formed on the assessment timeframe and initial officer feedback. |
| **Contract documentation** | Preliminaries, special conditions, tender returnable schedules, draft responsibility matrix | Draft the post-consent responsibility matrix during DD; issue late under addendum. |
| **Tender / award** | Tender Evaluation Committee (TEC), non-price then price stages, clarifications, interview, Tender Recommendation Report | Operate the TEC under formal procurement rules; produce the TRR; manage Letter-of-Award through procurement and legal. |
| **Contract administration** | PAP / Superintendent role under GC21 / equivalent; or Principal's Representative under bespoke CM agreement | Distinguish *Agent* from *Certifier* roles explicitly. Prepare a Contract Administration Procedures Manual (CAPM) per contract. |
| **Commissioning & handover** | Fabric and operational commissioning, FFE Group 1/2 integration, IT/HWAN, completion notice | Recognise that commissioning consumes elevated attention for specialist equipment (medical, hospitality, retail fitout). |
| **Defects liability / post-completion warranty** | Defects oversight, minor works, lessons learned | Resource for low but non-zero presence; an Assistant PM with defecting experience and field-management tools is the typical pattern. |

## 6. Governance & Interface Management

When two or more parties have skin in the design (co-developers, joint
venturers, prime tenants, government health agencies + LHDs), the PM's
real product is *interface management*. Recurring moves:

- **Map the governance layers**: Executive Steering Committee → Project
  Control / Planning & Development Committee → Project Delivery Team /
  Project Planning Team. Different decisions belong at different layers;
  the PM enforces the discipline.
- **Be Secretariat for the highest committee** to control the agenda and
  the record.
- **Demand a single point of contact** on the client side for each
  stream of input (clinical, commercial, FFE, IT). Multiple voices into
  the consultant team is the root cause of design churn.
- **Brief in on the controlling commercial agreement** (Development
  Agreement, Partition Agreement, Stratum Management Agreement) before
  drafting the PM's Project Delivery Plan — so the PDP does not create
  breach exposure.
- **Distinguish strategic governance from technical / commercial
  interface**: strategic interface is the client's; the PM supports
  technical / commercial interface (design meetings, RFI process, change
  control, utility / authority meetings).
- **Behavioural framing**: a recurring sentence is that successful joint
  delivery requires "a high performance team culture that, while
  considering its respective objectives, navigates a best-for-project
  outcome with transparent and honest consideration when there is a lack
  of alignment." This is doctrine, not filler.

## 7. Time, Scope, Cost, Quality, Risk — Five Live Registers

The PM commits to maintaining live registers across the five management
disciplines:

### 7.1 Time

- *Agreed Programme* established at outset, derived from the client's
  reference programme. Status monthly.
- Specialist in-house construction planner reviews the critical path,
  scheduling assumptions, and the contractor's construction methodology
  programmes (tender and post-contract).
- Re-sequencing and mitigation strategies are *offered*, not imposed.

### 7.2 Scope

- Project objectives + quality expectations + design intent + feasibility
  assumptions → consolidated and used as the *control* for change.
- For residential: an apartment-mix Schedule of Accommodation; quality of
  interior fixtures and finishes locked early to inform display-suite
  and sales-contract collateral.
- For commercial: NLA-control Schedule of Accommodation; Tenancy Fitout
  Guide drafted with the client.
- A **Change Control Register** is maintained for each stratum / scope
  separately.

### 7.3 Cost

- Independent QS / Cost Manager appointed (co-funded where applicable).
- PM does the cost reporting, budget reconciliation, contingency advice,
  consultant payments, contractor progress claim certification.
- Variations above a threshold (~$10k) escalate to the independent QS;
  alternatively the PM's own in-house QS supports.
- The PM identifies *cost flags* at concept stage — particularly where a
  call on executive contingency may become necessary — and signals
  pre-emptively.

### 7.4 Quality

- Project Quality Plan as the umbrella; consultant and contractor
  organisational quality plans nested beneath.
- **Display suite** for residential / sales-driven projects is a
  separate early-works package built off-site.
- **Prototype rooms** (kitchens, bathrooms, key clinical rooms) built
  early or off-site for coordination, detailing, and interface
  validation.
- Construction-phase Quality Plan addressing witness points, inspection
  points, samples, prototypes, and submissions.
- Where consultants are novated to the D&C contractor, a *peer-review*
  consultant team is retained.
- Monthly statement of compliance with PPR / current brief.

### 7.5 Risk

- A formal Risk Strategy outlined in the proposal, with sample entries
  in a structured format: **Risk → Cause → Consequence / Likelihood →
  Mitigating Action**.
- Reporting forums named: ESC monthly, PCG monthly with the full risk
  register as appendix, executive risk portal quarterly.
- Cost manager prepares a supplementary cost report addressing financial
  risks.

## 8. Resourcing — Transparent, Defensible, Bid-Back

Resourcing is treated as a *priced argument*, not a flat fee:

- **Bid back the indicative programme** as the basis for fee derivation —
  state explicitly that fee is conditional on programme stability and
  reserve the right to revisit if the programme moves materially.
- **Resource schedule provided as an appendix** showing monthly
  percentage allocation per named person, summed to FTE per month and
  total hours over the commission.
- **Leveraged team pyramid**: a Project Director (oversight, governance,
  legal interface), one or two Senior PMs (day-to-day project lead),
  PM (day-to-day support, on-site presence), Assistant / Cadet PM
  (QA/QC, defecting, supporting tasks). The pyramid is sized to the
  intensity of the phase.
- **Specialist heads available but excluded from base fee**: planning &
  programming specialist, commercial / claims specialist, in-house QS.
  Available on request.
- **Hourly rates published** by role for variations — typically with a
  stated annual indexation rule (e.g. 3% at 30 June compounding, cadet
  rates indexed higher to track market entry-level growth).
- **Phase-by-phase minimum-hour matrix** for fixed-scope government
  tenders, with a totalled minimum-hours summary.
- **Disbursements default to nil** where attendance is assumed all on
  one site; flagged with the qualifying assumption.

## 9. Key Personnel — Three Things Each Bio Must Prove

Each CV / personnel paragraph in a strong proposal is engineered to
prove three things:

1. **Direct, current, recent experience** with the *specific type* of
   project (sector, scale, complexity, contracting form).
2. **A relevant credential or reference** the evaluator can validate — a
   named referee with phone number, a prior project with comparable
   contractual structure (e.g. GC21 PAP work for a GC21 tender),
   familiarity with the specific authority / agency processes.
3. **Defined role on this commission** — what they personally will do,
   what they will lead, what they will hand to whom. No vague "support".

CVs include:

- Career chronology with sector, project value, role title.
- Specialisations list (focused, 6–10 bullets) — phrased as the kind of
  judgement calls the person can make, not generic activities.
- Industry sectors served.
- Formal qualifications and contract / standards literacy (e.g. GC21
  Edition 2; Section J energy reporting; ISO 9001).

## 10. Capability, Capacity, Continuity

Three distinct claims, each made separately:

- **Capability** — can the team do it? (Section refers back to CVs.)
- **Capacity** — are the people *available now* in the volumes
  required? Demonstrated by a current-commitments table listing each
  key person, their current projects, percentage of time committed,
  and expected completion dates.
- **Continuity / redundancy** — what happens if someone is on leave or
  leaves the firm? A short-term alternate (peer within the team) and a
  medium-term alternate (deeper in the business) named for each key
  person, with the qualifier that this is *not* an intention to
  substitute, but a continuity plan.

## 11. Commercial Position & Risk Allocation

Recurring commercial moves the PM makes — typically presented as
*items for discussion* rather than dealbreakers, framed against
specific clauses:

- **Payment terms** — typically 28 days from monthly invoice; some
  fee schedules invoice fortnightly.
- **Limitation of liability** — capped at a percentage of fees actually
  paid (50%, or 100% of contract sum in higher-risk roles), except
  where liability cannot be excluded by law. Backed by a worked example
  clause in an appendix.
- **Consequential loss exclusion** — separate clause excluding indirect
  / consequential losses (loss of use, data, profit, revenue, business,
  anticipated saving, goodwill, reputation, financing costs).
- **Proportionate liability** — the PM typically declines to contract
  out of proportionate-liability legislation because PI insurance does
  not support it.
- **Professional indemnity insurance** — a stated PI cover (commonly
  $10m with one reinstatement) and a parallel quote for a higher cover
  if mandated, with a fee uplift estimate.
- **Extension-of-time entitlements** — explicitly seek EoT for events
  outside reasonable control (including contractor actions affecting
  the PM's scope), and a corresponding fee variation where intensity
  of services genuinely changes — not at sole discretion of the
  Principal.
- **Variations** — fee may be varied by written notice from the PM for
  scope increase, cost increase outside PM's control, or scheduled
  annual rate review. Variation rate basis disclosed (hourly rates
  per role).
- **Disbursements** — typically actual cost plus 10% handling.
- **Insurance regime** — public liability stated (commonly $10m default
  if unspecified), workers comp statutory, plus any project-specific.
- **Non-solicitation** — staff non-poach clause with liquidated damages
  (typically 25% of remuneration package) for breach within 6 months
  of completion.
- **Dispute resolution** — staged: notice → confer within 5 business
  days → 28-day window → UNCITRAL arbitration in Sydney (or jurisdiction
  per schedule), English language, one arbitrator.
- **IP** — PM retains copyright; client granted a non-transferable
  royalty-free licence for the Commission upon payment of fee.

## 12. Recurring Site / Project Risk Themes

Patterns of risk that recur across very different project archetypes
and which the PM is expected to surface in the bid:

- **Ground plane / basement / flooding** — interaction of basement
  excavation, on-site detention, overland flood paths, regional
  stormwater upgrades, and authority risk-aversion around
  approving flood-affected developments. Often the single most
  budget-consuming issue in a small site in a flood-prone precinct.
- **Contamination & UST removal** — particularly on former service
  station or commercial sites; site auditor advice, interim audit
  advice for subdivision, environmental clearance sequencing.
- **Heritage** — facade retention, false-work, localised strengthening,
  preservation of identified fabric, two-stage DA with design
  competition, design excellence pathways.
- **Site access & materials handling** — crane locations, loading
  zones, air rights, structural strengthening to permit hoists,
  materials lift across adjacent properties, tower-crane repositioning.
- **Adjoining owners** — boundary condition resolution, party-wall
  consultation, dilapidation surveys, the limits of cooperation
  available from a neighbour competing for the same trades market.
- **Long-lead procurement** — structural steel, glazing, facade,
  specialist equipment (medical, vertical transportation). Lead-time
  forces overall duration.
- **Tenancy / operational disruption** — staging negotiated with
  tenants; shutdown windows in the PPR; coordination of trading peaks
  (Spring / Christmas / academic year).
- **Authority risk** — DA assessment duration unknown; conditions
  imposed late may not align with the forecast consent matrix used at
  tender; clause 4.6 / merit-assessment pathways needed for height
  / FSR exceedance.
- **COVID-style market shocks** — supply chain, escalation, procurement
  price volatility — addressed by benchmarking tender returns through
  peer-review processes and adjusting design / scope early.

## 13. The "Advantage" Narrative

Where the PM firm has sister disciplines (planning & programming,
commercial / construction consulting, quantity surveying), the proposal
frames these as a packaged advantage — services available on request,
priced separately if engaged, but standing behind the core PM team. The
sister-discipline expertise is referenced in the body of the
methodology so the client understands *which problem* it would be
called on to solve.

Where the firm is single-discipline, the equivalent move is to name
the specialist *sub-consultants* it intends to procure (cost manager,
flood consultant, facade consultant, etc.) so the client sees the
ecosystem the PM will assemble.

## 14. Authority & Stakeholder Mapping

A strong bid names the authorities and stakeholders that will shape
the project, not just the contractor and consultants:

- The relevant local planning authority — pre-application strategy,
  conditions matrix, late-DA contingency, design-competition vs merit-
  assessment pathway.
- Statutory referral agencies — water authority, energy distributor,
  gas, comms, transport agency, rail-adjacent works approvals.
- Heritage / Aboriginal heritage / environmental regulator.
- Adjoining owners and tenants — separately listed.
- Internal client governance — board, executive, senior delegates,
  procurement, legal, asset / operations representatives.
- For public-sector commissions: program steering committee, sponsoring
  ministry / department, end-user agency (e.g. local health district),
  treasury / cost-plan approver.

The PM's role is to maintain this stakeholder map and to manage the
*sequencing of consultations* — particularly authority pre-applications
relative to design milestones.

## 15. Concept-Phase Discipline (The Highest-Leverage Phase)

The concept phase is where the proposal asserts the PM earns its fee.
Recurring discipline:

- Hold a *Kick-Start* meeting with the client lead and cost manager
  *together* to align on budget tension before consultant work begins.
- Procure / confirm the missing core consultants identified as gaps.
- Front-load critical investigations (geotech, contamination, flood,
  utility capacity, traffic) — without these, options analysis is
  speculative.
- Run options analysis tightly with the architect; produce a concept
  cost plan and value-management response for each option.
- Engage the planning authority in pre-application *before* the
  preferred option is locked.
- Flag, in writing, any need to call executive contingency at concept
  stage — never leave it to a later milestone.
- Endorse the concept design only after authority input has been
  factored.

## 16. Procurement-Phase Discipline

- Tender Evaluation Plan signed off before tenders are issued.
- Tender period managed including site briefing, RFI cycle, addenda.
- Non-price evaluation first by the TEC, *blind to price*; consensus
  ranking achieved before price is opened.
- Price evaluation introduced via the cost manager's commercial report.
- Final clarifications / interview / preferred-tenderer interview.
- Tender Recommendation Report drafted by the PM, endorsed by
  procurement, approved by the delegate.
- Letter of Award reviewed by procurement and legal before issue.
- Contract Administration Procedures Manual drafted for each contract
  awarded.

---

## 17. Construction-Phase Discipline

- PM acts as Principal's Authorised Person / Principal's Representative
  / Superintendent per the contract; role disclosed (Agent vs Certifier)
  in the CAPM.
- Specialist planning & programming support reviews the contractor's
  detailed construction programme and tracks delay events.
- Specialist commercial support assesses variations and EOT claims.
- Witness / inspection / hold points administered per the project
  Quality Plan.
- Monthly compliance statement issued (compliance of works with PPR /
  brief).
- Site presence calibrated to phase intensity — elevated during facade,
  fitout, services commissioning, defecting.

---

## 18. Completion, Commissioning, Defects

- Fabric commissioning precedes operational commissioning; both run in
  parallel toward a completion notice with the intent of minimal /
  negligible defects at handover.
- Group 1 FFE / Group 2 FFE distinguished where the contract or program
  uses that taxonomy; Group 2 may require attendance during base
  building works.
- Specialist IT / data / services (e.g. clinical IT, hospitality MEP)
  needs early resolution and dedicated stakeholder consultation through
  the LHD / asset / IT representative as applicable.
- Defects liability period staffed with at least one assistant PM
  experienced in defecting; field-management software (e.g.
  Aconex Field or equivalent) named.
- Lessons-learned / post-occupancy review built into the post-completion
  warranty period.

## 19. Proposal Document Architecture (Section Order That Recurs)

For a non-government client RFP:

1. Cover letter restating the opportunity.
2. Executive summary / value proposition.
3. Conflicts of interest disclosure.
4. Returnable schedules (fee, key personnel, hourly rates).
5. Procurement & delivery strategy (the strategic core).
6. Phase-by-phase methodology — design, procurement, construction.
7. Risk, time, scope, cost, quality management approaches.
8. Past projects with named references and contractual structure.
9. Company QA / management systems.
10. Current commissions / capacity / continuity.
11. Mark-up of the client's draft PM Agreement clauses with commercial
    departures grouped.
12. Appendices: programme, resource schedule, CVs, accreditation,
    insurance certificates, example clauses (limitation of liability,
    indirect loss).

For a government tender:

1. Tender form (deed-poll execution).
2. Lump sum fee + breakdown by program part (e.g. Parts 0–9).
3. Rates for variations.
4. Non-compliances and qualifications.
5. Project plan — understanding, methodology, communication, coordination,
   tender programme.
6. Experience of key personnel (with maximum-page limits).
7. Organisational experience — sample similar commissions with named
   client referees.
8. Risk management strategy (table format).
9. Resource schedule (minimum hours per phase, per person).
10. Management systems (QMS, EMS, OHSMS) with accreditation certificates.
11. Insurance certificates of currency.
12. CVs as attachments.

## 20. Cross-Cutting Bid Patterns Worth Remembering

- **Specificity beats fluency**. Naming the prior project, the prior
  authority officer, the actual clause number, the actual lead-time
  in weeks — these are the signals an evaluator picks up.
- **Make every methodology choice defensible**. "We do X" is weak.
  "We do X because of market condition Y, risk Z, and the timing
  constraint W" is strong.
- **Don't pretend the design is more complete than it is**. State the
  documentation level and the consultants still missing.
- **Build the staging argument from the operating environment**.
  Tenant trading peaks, academic year, clinical service interruptions,
  authority shut-down constraints — the staging logic should reflect
  the *client's business*, not the contractor's convenience.
- **Hold continuous risk language**. Cause → Consequence → Likelihood →
  Mitigation is the unit of risk discussion; reuse it everywhere.
- **Show the leveraged pyramid**. Director / Senior PM / PM / Assistant
  PM with named hourly rates and clear role demarcation.
- **Be candid about commercial markups on the PM Agreement**. Identify
  the clauses, explain the rationale, propose alternatives, attach
  example clauses. Don't bury reservations in side letters.
- **Differentiate Agent from Certifier explicitly**. The contract
  administration role under bespoke or GC21 contracts hinges on it.
- **Treat the Service Delivery Plan / Project Delivery Plan as a
  contractual deliverable**, not boilerplate. It captures the
  governance, communication, quality, risk and document-control
  protocols.
- **Use shared appendices for commodity content** — limitation of
  liability example clause, indirect-loss clause, insurance
  certificates — but tailor the strategic sections to the project.
