### Current Market Conditions (2025-2026)

**Facade Systems:**
- Unitised curtain wall is now the dominant system for commercial/residential towers >6 storeys in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane. Stick curtain wall remains for smaller commercial/mixed-use.
- Chinese-manufactured unitised systems have significant market share (~40-50% of commercial towers by value in eastern seaboard capitals), with Australian fabricators (Permasteelisa, G James, Yuanda AU, Inhabit) competing on quality, warranty, and local support.
- Post-combustible cladding crisis: ACP (aluminium composite panel) with PE core is effectively banned for buildings >2 storeys. FR-grade ACP (A2/B1 fire-rated) or non-combustible alternatives (solid aluminium, zinc, terracotta, fibre cement) now standard.
- Lead times for unitised curtain wall systems: 20-30 weeks from shop drawing approval to first delivery. This is the single longest lead item in most multi-storey fit-out programs.

**Glazing & Windows:**
- Double glazing is now mandatory in most climate zones under NCC 2024 Section J energy provisions. This has increased cost by $80-$150/m2 over single glazing for residential.
- AWA certification is increasingly specified by tier-1 builders and government projects.
- Aluminium framing dominates (>85% market share). uPVC growing in residential, particularly in VIC and cooler climates. Timber/aluminium composite remains a niche premium product.

**Waterproofing:**
- The single largest defect category in Australian construction. QBCC data consistently shows waterproofing/wet area defects comprising 25-35% of all building defect complaints.
- Torch-on modified bitumen remains dominant for external/podium/roof applications.
- Liquid-applied polyurethane membranes (Wolfin, Sika, Tremco) gaining share for complex geometry and planter/podium applications.
- Internal wet areas: acrylic-modified cementitious membranes dominate the residential market. Two-part polyurethane and epoxy systems used in commercial wet areas where higher performance is required.
- AIW certified applicator program growing but not yet mandatory in any state. Advocacy continues for licensing of waterproofing as a specialist trade (currently anyone with a builder's licence can waterproof in most states).

**Joinery & Cabinetry:**
- Supply chain has stabilised post-COVID but lead times remain elevated vs 2019 levels.
- Imported cabinetry (primarily from China and Vietnam) holds significant market share in residential apartments and spec-grade commercial fitouts.
- Domestic fabrication commands a 30-50% price premium but offers tighter dimensional control and faster defect rectification.
- Stone benchtop supply: engineered stone (Caesarstone, Silestone, Essastone) under regulatory pressure due to silicosis risk. SafeWork bans on dry cutting in all states. Some builders shifting to porcelain slab (Dekton, Neolith) as an alternative.

**Prefabricated Bathroom Pods:**
- Growing adoption in hotel, student accommodation, and build-to-rent sectors.
- Australian manufacturers: Interpod, Moduler, Hickory (Melbourne-based).
- Typical cost premium of 5-15% over traditional construction, offset by 30-40% program reduction for the wet area scope.
- Key PM challenge: early design lock-in (pod design must be finalised at DA/CC stage, not deferred to construction as is common with traditional bathrooms).
- Interface with structure is critical: setdown tolerances, structural loading, and plumbing/electrical rough-in coordination must be resolved months before pod delivery.

**Internal Partitions & Ceilings:**
- Plasterboard supply dominated by CSR (Gyprock), Knauf, and BGC. Supply is currently stable.
- AWCI fire-rated wall system specifications are the standard reference for fire-rated construction.
- Acoustic performance requirements tightened under NCC 2024 for multi-residential — driving demand for resilient mounting, acoustic insulation, and proprietary wall/ceiling systems (Rw + Ctr ratings).

**Painting & Coatings:**
- VOC regulations tightening in all states — low-VOC products now standard for interior applications.
- Intumescent coating demand increased due to exposed structural steel in commercial/mixed-use design trends. Lead times for intumescent coating application: 4-8 weeks (requires certified applicator).

### Recent Industry Shifts

1. **Combustible cladding crisis (2017-ongoing):** Post-Grenfell (UK) and Lacrosse (Melbourne), every state established cladding audit and rectification programs. Estimated $6B+ national rectification liability. NCC 2019/2022/2024 progressively tightened facade material requirements. Major shift to non-combustible facade systems.

2. **NCC 2024 energy efficiency (Section J):** Step-change in thermal performance requirements for commercial buildings. Facade U-values, glazing solar heat gain coefficients (SHGC), and airtightness now significantly impact facade design and cost. Double glazing effectively mandatory in climate zones 2-8.

3. **Silicosis crisis — engineered stone:** SafeWork Australia prohibition on uncontrolled dry cutting of engineered stone. NSW, VIC, QLD have implemented or announced bans on engineered stone benchtop fabrication without strict dust controls. Some jurisdictions moving towards complete bans. PMs must verify stone supplier compliance with WHS requirements.

4. **Prefabricated/modular adoption:** Bathroom pods, prefab riser modules, and pre-finished wall panels gaining traction in repetitive building types (apartments, hotels, student accommodation). Requires earlier design freeze and different procurement sequencing.

5. **Acoustic performance tightening:** NCC 2024 increased acoustic separation requirements for Class 2 (apartment) buildings. Impact STC (Rw + Ctr) requirements now 50+ for inter-tenancy walls and floors. Driving use of proprietary acoustic systems and more careful detailing of flanking paths.

### NCC 2024 Key Provisions Affecting Architectural Trades

| NCC Provision | Impact on Architectural Trades |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| **C1.9 / C1.9a — Non-combustible building elements** | External walls, facade systems must use non-combustible materials or achieve tested compliance for buildings Type A/B construction (generally >2 storeys). Drives facade material selection. |
| **C1.10 — Fire hazard properties of materials** | Internal lining materials must comply with spread-of-flame and smoke-developed indices. Impacts wall linings, ceiling tiles, insulation selection. |
| **C2.5-C2.7 — Fire-resistance of building elements** | Prescribes FRL for walls, floors, columns by building classification and type of construction. Directly drives fire-rated partition, shaft, and floor specifications. |
| **C3.15 — Openings in fire-resistant construction** | Penetration sealing and construction joint requirements. Every pipe, duct, cable, and service that passes through a fire-rated element must be sealed to maintain the FRL. |
| **C4.2 — Specification for fire doors** | Fire door rating, installation, and hardware requirements. Annual inspection obligations. |
| **F1.7 — Waterproofing and damp-proofing** | Mandatory waterproofing of wet areas, external waterproofed areas. References AS 3740, AS 4654. |
| **F5.5 — Sound insulation for Class 2/3** | Airborne sound (Rw + Ctr >= 50 inter-tenancy), impact sound (Ln,w <= 62). Flanking path management is the PM's coordination challenge. |
| **Section J — Energy efficiency** | Building envelope thermal performance (U-values, R-values), glazing performance (U-value, SHGC), building sealing. Drives facade, glazing, insulation, and sealing specifications. |
| **D3.2-D3.3 — Stairways and balustrades** | Balustrade height (1.0m general, 1.0m stairs), opening size (125mm max), structural loading (0.75 kN/m horizontal). Drives balustrade system selection. |

### Defect & Failure Register

Architectural trades consistently represent the largest share of building defect complaints in Australia. QBCC data (2022-2024) shows waterproofing/wet area defects at 25-35% of all complaints. VBA data shows similar patterns. The following register captures the 18 most significant failure modes.

| # | Failure Mode | Trade | Typical Cause | Remediation Cost Range | Prevention Strategy |
|---|-------------|-------|--------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Wet area waterproofing failure — shower/bathroom** | Waterproofing | Membrane puncture during tiling, inadequate upturns (<150mm), failure at floor waste junction, no bond-breaker at corners, membrane applied over damp substrate | $8,000-$35,000 per bathroom (complete strip-out and re-waterproof). Multi-bathroom apartments: $50,000-$150,000. Strata claims for multi-level water damage: $200,000-$2,000,000+ | AS 3740 compliant membrane application by trained applicator. Mandatory flood testing (24hr, 200mm depth) BEFORE tiling. Independent waterproofing inspection at pre-tile stage. Bond-breaker tape at all internal corners/junctions. Minimum 72hr cure before flood test. Photographic record of completed membrane. |
| 2 | **Balcony/podium waterproofing failure** | Waterproofing | Inadequate falls (<1:100), blocked/undersized drainage, membrane failure at balustrade/wall junctions, no overflow provision, thermal cycling degradation of membrane | $15,000-$80,000 per balcony (scaffold, strip tiles, re-waterproof, re-tile). Podium-level failure affecting below-ground areas: $200,000-$1,500,000+ | Design to AS 4654.1 — minimum 1:100 falls (1:60 preferred), overflow scuppers, step-down at door thresholds (minimum 75mm, 150mm preferred). ### Key Defect Patterns — PM Implications

1. **Waterproofing is the #1 risk.** Across all Australian state building authorities, waterproofing/wet area defects consistently represent the single largest complaint category (25-35% of all building defect complaints per QBCC). The remediation-to-prevention cost ratio is approximately 20:1 — every $1,000 spent on proper waterproofing inspection and testing prevents $20,000+ in remediation costs.

2. **Fire-rated construction is the #2 hidden risk.** Non-compliance is often invisible (sealed inside walls/ceilings) and only discovered during fire safety audits, insurance inspections, or actual fire events. Post-Grenfell regulatory scrutiny means enforcement is increasing.

3. **Acoustic defects are the fastest-growing complaint category** in multi-residential construction, driven by NCC 2024 tightening requirements and increasing occupant expectations. Acoustic defects are extremely expensive to remediate because they require strip-out of completed finishes.

4. **Facade defects have the highest individual remediation cost** due to access costs (scaffold/rope access) and the scale of affected area.

### Program Sequencing — Internal Finishes (Critical Path Dependencies)

This is the sequence that PMs must manage daily on every multi-storey project. Each step is dependent on the prior step's completion and inspection approval.

```
1. Structure complete (slab, columns, shear walls)
     |
2. External facade/windows installed → WEATHERTIGHT milestone
     |
3. Fire-rated shaft walls and inter-tenancy walls framed (one side lined)
     |
4. MEP first-fix rough-in (pipes, ducts, cables, conduit IN the walls/ceiling zone)
     |
5. Waterproofing of wet areas → HOLD POINT: inspect + flood test
     |
6. Fire-rated wall second-side lining → HOLD POINT: inspect before close-up
     |
7. Ceiling grid installation (after above-ceiling services tested and inspected)
     |
8. Wall tiling in wet areas (over approved waterproofing)
     |
9. Floor screeding/levelling (if required for floor finishes)
     |
10. Joinery & cabinetry installation
     |
11. Door hanging and hardware installation
     |
12. Painting (3-coat system after all trades complete in area)
     |
13. Floor finishes (carpet, vinyl, timber — LAST trade to protect from damage)
     |
14. MEP final-fix (GPOs, switches, diffusers, sprinkler heads through ceiling)
     |
15. Painting touch-up and clean
     |
16. Defect inspection and rectification
     |
17. Practical Completion
```

**Critical interfaces where PMs lose weeks:**
- Step 2→3: Facade delay pushes entire internal fit-out. Every week of facade delay = approximately 0.8 weeks of handover delay (not 1:1 due to some overlap possible).
- Step 4→5→6: MEP rough-in must be 100% complete in wet area walls before waterproofing. Late services changes punch through completed waterproofing ($2,000-$5,000 per bathroom to re-waterproof).
- Step 5 hold point: Flood testing takes 24-48 hours. 200 bathrooms at 10 per day = 4 weeks of flood testing program. Must be sequenced with waterproofing crew production rate.
- Step 10→11→12: Joinery and doors must be installed before painting to avoid damage. But painting cannot start until joinery is complete. If joinery is late, painting is late, handover is late.
