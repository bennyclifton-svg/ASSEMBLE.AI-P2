# Client-Side PM Commissions Guide

## 1. The Client-Side PM Commission

### Commission Definition and Boundaries
A client-side project management commission is the appointment of an independent project manager (PM) to represent the Principal (developer, owner, sponsor) across some or all phases of the project lifecycle. Unlike the contractor's site-based PM, the client-side PM owes a single duty of care to the Principal and stewards the Principal's time, cost, scope, quality and risk objectives.

A well-defined commission states the phases included (e.g. inception through DLP), the role on each phase (Project Director, lead PM, support PM, Contract Administrator / Superintendent / Principal's Representative), the deliverables (Project Delivery Plan, monthly reports, registers, compliance statements), and the scope explicitly excluded (specialist services like QS, scheduling, claims analysis).

Reference: Industry best practice (AIQS / RICS guidelines; AIPM)
Applies to: All project archetypes; intensity calibrated to phase complexity.
Common pitfalls:
- Accepting a vague brief that does not delineate Agent vs Certifier role; this surfaces later as a contract administration ambiguity.
- Including specialist scope (claims, dispute, in-depth scheduling, QS) in the lump sum without resourcing it separately.
- Failing to anchor the fee to a specific reference programme — any programme drift then forces a fee variation conversation under stress.

### Five Questions a PM Proposal Must Answer
A strong PM proposal answers five questions for the Principal: (1) do you understand what I am trying to do and the constraints I am under; (2) what is the procurement and contracting strategy and why is it right for this market and risk position right now; (3) how will you organise the work to protect my interests across time, cost, scope, quality and risk; (4) who is doing the work, can they actually deliver it, and do they have capacity now; (5) what will it cost, on what basis, and how is risk allocated between us commercially.

A weak proposal merely describes services. A strong proposal makes arguments — every methodology choice is justified by reference to project risk, market conditions, or Principal objectives.

Reference: Industry practice in client-side PM bidding.
Applies to: All competitive PM appointments, private and public.
Common pitfalls:
- Generic methodology copy that does not engage with the specific project's risk profile.
- Resourcing claims unsupported by a current commitments disclosure and continuity plan.
- Fee offered without a referenced programme, making any programme drift a fee-variation flashpoint.

### The Service Delivery Plan as Foundational Deliverable
The Service Delivery Plan (SDP) — also called the Project Delivery Plan — is the PM's primary post-appointment deliverable. It captures the governance structure, communication protocols, register conventions, change control thresholds, quality plan, risk management approach, and document control practices the PM will administer.

The SDP is drafted with reference to the controlling commercial agreements between client parties (Development Agreement, Partition Agreement, Stratum Management Agreement, Joint Venture deed) so the PM's process does not create breach exposure. Each core consultant is then asked to prepare an aligned SDP; the PM consolidates them into a single coordinated SDP for the Project.

Reference: HI Service Delivery Plan template (NSW Health Infrastructure GC21 commissions); AIPM PM body of knowledge.
Applies to: All commissions with multi-party governance or formal consultant teams.
Common pitfalls:
- Drafting the SDP before being briefed on the controlling commercial agreements.
- Treating the SDP as a one-off document; it must be revised at major phase gates.
- Not having consultants' SDPs aligned, leaving gaps in document control and approvals.

## 2. Establishing Project Understanding

### Document Review and Pre-Bid Diligence
The opening section of a strong proposal is not a summary; it is evidence of comprehension. The PM names the documents reviewed — Statement of Environmental Effects, applicant's construction management plan, consultant design programme, Development Agreement, partition agreements, clinical service plans, models of care — and demonstrates awareness of the staging logic those documents imply.

The PM restates the scope in their own words, identifies which stage is most complex and why (typically driven by long-lead procurement, shop-drawing risk, deconstruction/reconstruction interfaces, or critical-path tenant interfaces), and notes any scope explicitly excluded with a rationale (e.g. excluded because outside the time frame for the trading deadline).

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: Proposal preparation and project mobilisation.
Common pitfalls:
- Citing documents that were not actually reviewed — easily exposed at interview.
- Restating scope verbatim from the brief, signalling no independent analysis.
- Excluding scope without a stated reason, which reads as deflection rather than discipline.

### Programme Translation and Critical-Path Identification
Where the Principal provides a reference programme, the PM translates it into their own indicative programme and presents the row-by-row comparison. If the PM can credibly shave duration through realistic consent assumptions, concurrent activities, or sequencing improvements, the differences are tabled (e.g. PM Tender Programme Completion vs Brief Completion).

Identifying the *true* critical path early — typically through long-lead trades (structural steel, glazing, facade), authority consent, tenant decant, or specialist commissioning — informs both the fee derivation and the procurement strategy.

Reference: Industry practice; PMBOK schedule management.
Applies to: All PM commissions with a Principal-supplied reference programme.
Common pitfalls:
- Accepting the reference programme without interrogation, then being trapped by its assumptions.
- Compressing duration without naming the specific assumptions (consent period, decant period, lead-time recovery) that enable the compression.

## 3. Procurement Strategy as the Strategic Core

### Matching Contracting Form to Market and Design Completeness
Procurement strategy is treated as the strategic core of a PM proposal, not a procedural appendix. In a busy contractor market, removing trade-pricing risk from the contractor makes the package attractive: Construction Management with open-book trade pricing (Principal carries trade-pricing risk until trades are let as sub-contracts) is a tool to attract competitive bidders when the design is high-resolution but tender risk is otherwise unappealing.

Where the project supports fuller risk transfer (well-resolved design, less complex interface), GC21-style or D&C arrangements with full design-finalisation risk on the contractor become viable. The fee structure of a CM contract (lump-sum preliminaries, overheads and margin against a PPR with a forecast consent responsibility matrix) is itself the procurement lever.

Reference: Industry practice; AS 4000-1997, AS 4902-2000, GC21 (NSW Government).
Applies to: All procurement strategy advisory work.
Common pitfalls:
- Defaulting to D&C or lump-sum because it is familiar, regardless of market conditions or design completeness.
- Failing to make the lump-sum preliminaries component clean, so trade preliminaries duplicate it.
- Transferring design risk the market will not price competitively.

### The Principal's Project Requirements (PPR)
The PPR is the central tender document; design drawings sit beneath it. PPR contents that recur: a forecast consent matrix (anticipated conditions and where responsibility for each will sit); order of works and staging plan accounting for tenant negotiations and authority-imposed shutdown windows; site management protocol, materials handling, access constraints; handover and completion gateways; quality, WHS and IR expectations.

A clean separation of design documents (by the consultants), contract documents (by the Principal's legal counsel), and PPR (by the PM) is the operational discipline.

Reference: NSW Government GC21 PPR conventions; industry best practice for D&C and CM contracts.
Applies to: D&C, ECI, CM and Managing Contractor procurements; any contracting form where design or pricing is finalised post-award.
Common pitfalls:
- Burying critical staging or shutdown constraints in design drawings rather than in the PPR.
- Drafting a PPR that lists "outcomes" without specifying performance criteria or quality standards.
- Failing to update the PPR when authority consent conditions arrive post-tender.

### The Forecast Consent Matrix
When authority consent is not in hand at tender time, the PM prepares a forecast consent matrix — the team's best read of likely conditions and where responsibility for each will sit. The matrix is used as the tender basis; the post-consent variance from it is then the trigger for transparent commercial negotiation with the appointed contractor.

This device allows the procurement to be advanced ahead of consent, preserving programme — which can be critical when the project must hit a trading peak, academic year start, or financial-year close.

Reference: Industry practice; commonly used in fast-track public-domain and retail-fitout works.
Applies to: All projects where DA assessment timing risks the contract award programme.
Common pitfalls:
- Forecast matrix prepared without genuine consultation with a planning consultant familiar with the assessing authority.
- Treating the matrix as authoritative once consent arrives, rather than as a baseline for variance-driven commercial negotiation.
- Not communicating to tenderers that the matrix is forecast, not consented.

### Shortlist-Based Tendering
A shortlist of 3–4 tenderers is the default for client-side PM-managed procurements. The shortlist is justified by capability, capacity, current commitment levels, and the specific risk profile of the project. The PM publishes the evaluation methodology (price + non-price weighting), manages the tender period including site briefing and RFI cycle, runs a structured Tender Evaluation Committee (TEC) process, and drafts the Tender Recommendation Report.

Non-price evaluation is conducted first by the TEC, blind to price; consensus ranking is achieved before price is opened.

Reference: NSW Procurement Rules; industry best practice (AIQS, RICS).
Applies to: All competitive procurements managed by client-side PMs.
Common pitfalls:
- Inviting tenderers without genuine capacity assessment, leading to declined invitations or weak responses.
- Allowing price information into the non-price evaluation, contaminating the consensus rank.
- Skipping the structured interview / clarification phase before Preferred Tenderer is declared.

### Alternate Procurement Routes (Other Contractor Reserves)
Where one party is positioned as the natural builder — a co-developer who is also a D&C contractor, or an incumbent on adjacent works — the PM prepares in parallel for an "Other Contractor" route. Documentation is prepared to a standard that enables a competitive tender if the natural builder's price is rejected. The interface and access penalties of switching builders are surfaced so the Principal can make the trade-off transparently.

Two-contractor-on-one-site logistics (crane locations, materials handling, sequencing) typically become the dominant cost of switching.

Reference: Industry practice; common in JV / co-developer structures.
Applies to: Multi-party developments where one party doubles as builder.
Common pitfalls:
- Not preparing the documentation in advance, leaving the Principal with no real alternative when the natural builder's price arrives.
- Underestimating the access constraints of bringing in a second contractor mid-programme.
- Failing to incorporate the alternate route into the Construction Management Plan.

### Critical-Path Trade Procurement Choreography
For complex packages — structural steel, glazing, facade, specialist medical or hospitality equipment — the chain of shop drawing → fabrication → logistics → installation typically drives the critical path. The PM ensures the contractor's trade procurement, shop-drawing reviews, and trade tender returns are reviewed in parallel with the cost manager / QS.

Lead-times on long-lead trades may force the overall duration of an early stage, which is most efficiently delivered as a coordinated work front rather than discontinuous packages.

Reference: Industry practice; specialist trade scheduling.
Applies to: Projects with significant long-lead manufactured elements.
Common pitfalls:
- Treating shop-drawing review as a contractor-only activity, missing early opportunities to flag detailing or coordination issues.
- Letting the trade procurement programme drift behind the head-contract programme.
- Discovering long-lead durations only after contract award.

## 4. Design Basis and Design Risk Allocation

### Calibrating Design Risk Transfer to Market Conditions
A full errors-and-omissions design-risk transfer (D&C with full design novation) is unlikely to yield competitive pricing when the design is complex — heritage envelopes, intricate facade, services interfaces. Better to invest Principal-side time advancing the design to high resolution and accept a narrower transfer ("design finalisation only" with the consultants retained Principal-side).

Reference: Industry practice; AS 4902 D&C conventions.
Applies to: All decisions about contract form vs design completeness.
Common pitfalls:
- Insisting on full design transfer for prestige or complex works in a tight market and receiving inflated or non-conforming bids.
- Novating consultants without proper indemnity transfer, breaking the chain of design liability.

### Identifying Missing Consultants on the Principal's Side
Before going to tender the PM identifies missing consultants on the Principal's side — typically structural engineer for complex retrofit, facade consultant for non-trivial glazing, hydraulic engineer for awning drainage and stormwater interface, flood consultant for basement design in flood-prone precincts, acoustic for sensitive uses.

Don't assume the existing team is complete; gaps surface as variations and delays post-contract.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All concept-to-tender phases.
Common pitfalls:
- Assuming the lead architect will cover specialist consulting areas (facade, hydraulic, fire engineering).
- Engaging specialist consultants too late, after their input could have shaped design options.

### Anticipating Future Stages in Current Design
Even if subsequent stages are deferred, ensure services routing, ground-plane decisions, and structural provisions in the current stage do not lock out future stages or force expensive rework. A future-stage check is performed before completing tender documentation on each stage.

Reference: Industry practice; staged developments.
Applies to: Multi-stage developments and incremental refurbishments.
Common pitfalls:
- Designing each stage in isolation, then discovering Stage 4 cannot proceed without disturbing Stage 1 finishes.
- Omitting services capacity provision for future tenancies or use changes.

## 5. Methodology by Phase

### Project Initiation and Mobilisation
A Kick-Start meeting is held with the Principal lead and cost manager together to align on budget tension before consultant work begins. Outstanding core consultants are procured. A draft Service Delivery Plan is prepared. A Project Initiation Workshop convenes all appointed consultants for alignment on objectives, process, programme, and consultant-level SDPs.

Reference: Industry practice; AIPM body of knowledge.
Applies to: All commission mobilisations.
Common pitfalls:
- Mobilising without the cost manager appointed, leaving budget tension unresolved at concept phase.
- Skipping the joint Kick-Start meeting; PM and cost manager then arrive at concept phase with mismatched assumptions.

### Feasibility and Functional Briefing
For public-sector or institutional projects, a structured functional briefing produces a Schedule of Accommodation. The PM delays endorsement of the functional brief / SoA until concept design, preliminary cost plan, and authority pre-application input have been folded in. Endorsing the brief in a vacuum undermines later phases.

For commercial projects, the equivalent is locking the apartment mix and unit schedule for residential, or the Net Lettable Area and Tenancy Fitout Guide for commercial, before tender design begins.

Reference: AusHFG (health facility planning); industry practice.
Applies to: All projects with formal briefing requirements (health, education, defence) and major commercial/residential.
Common pitfalls:
- Endorsing the functional brief before cost or planning context is known.
- Treating the brief as immutable when concept design reveals it is unaffordable or non-compliant.

### Concept Design (Highest-Leverage Phase)
The concept phase is where the PM earns the fee. Critical investigations are front-loaded (geotech, contamination, flood, utility capacity, traffic, ground plane, basement). Options analysis is run with the architect; each option carries a concept cost plan and value-management response. The planning authority is engaged in pre-application before the preferred option is locked. Any need to call executive contingency is flagged in writing at concept stage — never deferred to a later milestone.

Endorsement of the concept design occurs only after authority input has been factored.

Reference: Industry practice; planning consultant guidance.
Applies to: All projects with significant site or design uncertainty.
Common pitfalls:
- Running concept design without geotech, contamination or flood data.
- Locking the preferred option before pre-application authority feedback.
- Hiding a contingency call until schematic, by which time confidence is destroyed.

### Schematic Design and Development Approval
Pre-application authority consultation #2 occurs during schematic design. Specialist consultants for the Statement of Environmental Effects are procured. Clause 4.6 / merit-assessment pathways are planned for early rather than relying on design-competition concessions. Schematic design includes a cost plan, an HI ERG-equivalent peer review for public projects, and value management before HI / sponsor endorsement.

The Crown DA / standard DA / state significant development pathway is selected based on the asset class and gross floor area.

Reference: NSW EP&A Act 1979; SEPP suite; local DCPs; industry practice.
Applies to: Projects requiring DA / Crown DA consent.
Common pitfalls:
- Skipping pre-application meeting #2, relying on the first meeting's feedback.
- Lodging without working through draft consent conditions with the Crown / agency Review Officer.
- Underestimating exhibition-period response activity.

### Design Development
Design development typically starts after a view is formed on the assessment timeframe and once initial feedback is gained from the responsible officer. This mitigates nugatory work and variations if the design needs to be partially redirected arising authority technical review. Design development is on the critical path, so commencement should not be overly delayed.

DD activities are followed by a cost plan, peer review (where applicable), value engineering, and Principal endorsement; the cost plan becomes the Pre-Tender Estimate.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All major projects between schematic and tender.
Common pitfalls:
- Commencing DD prematurely, before authority direction is clear.
- Failing to issue a peer-reviewed cost plan before tender.

### Contract Documentation
Tender documentation includes Preliminaries, Agreement Information (for GC21 or equivalent), any amendment to special conditions, Tender Conditions and Returnable Schedules. A draft consent responsibility matrix is prepared during DD; the final responsibility matrix is anticipated late in the tender period, issued under addendum, or as a post-tender-close matter.

The PM briefs the Principal's procurement and legal teams on the proposed methodology and manages their review of the documentation.

Reference: GC21; AS 4000; AS 4902; ABIC suite.
Applies to: All competitive procurements.
Common pitfalls:
- Issuing tender documentation without procurement / legal sign-off.
- Drafting a responsibility matrix that does not match the contract form's mechanism for handling consent variations.

### Tender Evaluation and Award
A Tender Evaluation Plan is signed off before tenders issue. The tender period includes site briefing, RFI cycle, and addenda. Non-price evaluation by the TEC precedes price disclosure; consensus rank is achieved before price is opened. Price is introduced via the cost manager's commercial report. Clarifications and interview produce a Tender Recommendation Report drafted by the PM, endorsed by procurement, approved by the delegate. The Letter of Award is reviewed by procurement and legal before issue.

Reference: NSW Procurement Rules; industry practice.
Applies to: Competitive procurements with formal evaluation governance.
Common pitfalls:
- Allowing price into the non-price evaluation discussion.
- Producing a TRR that does not address each evaluation criterion explicitly.
- Issuing a Letter of Award without legal review where contract negotiations are still live.

### Contract Administration
The PM acts as Principal's Authorised Person (PAP) under GC21, as Principal's Representative or Superintendent under AS 4000 / AS 4902, or as Contract Administrator under bespoke CM agreements. The role (Agent vs Certifier) is disclosed in a Contract Administration Procedures Manual (CAPM) prepared per contract.

Specialist planning and programming support reviews the contractor's detailed construction programme; specialist commercial support assesses variations and EOT claims. Witness, inspection, and hold points are administered per the project Quality Plan. A monthly statement of compliance with PPR / current brief is issued.

Reference: GC21 (PAP); AS 4000 / AS 4902 (Superintendent); ABIC suite (Architect/CA); bespoke CM forms.
Applies to: All construction-phase administration.
Common pitfalls:
- Not preparing a CAPM, leading to ad hoc administration practices.
- Acting as Certifier when the contract requires Agent (or vice versa).
- Issuing monthly progress certificates without inspection or QS reconciliation.

### Commissioning and Handover
Fabric commissioning precedes operational commissioning; both run in parallel toward a Completion Notice with the intent of minimal / negligible defects at handover. Group 1 / Group 2 FFE is distinguished where the contract or programme uses that taxonomy; Group 2 FFE may require attendance during base building works.

Specialist services (clinical IT, hospitality MEP, retail tenancy fitout integration) need early resolution with dedicated stakeholder consultation through the asset / IT / operations representative.

Reference: GC21 commissioning provisions; AS 4000 / AS 4902 completion provisions; AusHFG commissioning requirements.
Applies to: All projects with specialist commissioning requirements.
Common pitfalls:
- Treating commissioning as a contractor-only activity, missing user acceptance test (UAT) inputs.
- Group 2 FFE attempted post-completion when it should have been integrated during base build.

### Defects Liability and Post-Completion
At least one assistant PM with defects experience is allocated to the defects liability period; field-management software (Aconex Field or equivalent) is named. The PM continues administering the contract through the warranty period.

A lessons-learned / post-occupancy review is built into the post-completion phase, particularly for institutional clients where the outcome informs the next project.

Reference: GC21; AS 4000; ABIC defects liability provisions.
Applies to: All commissions extending through DLP.
Common pitfalls:
- Under-resourcing the DLP phase, leading to drift in defect resolution.
- Not closing out the lessons-learned review, losing institutional knowledge.

## 6. Governance and Interface Management

### Multi-Party Governance Mapping
The governance layers are mapped at outset: Executive Steering Committee → Project Control / Planning & Development Committee → Project Delivery Team / Project Planning Team. Different decisions belong at different layers; the PM enforces the discipline.

Where possible, the PM acts as Secretariat to the highest committee to control the agenda and the record.

Reference: Industry practice; public-sector program governance.
Applies to: All multi-party and institutional projects.
Common pitfalls:
- Decisions made at the wrong governance layer, requiring later re-endorsement.
- PM not acting as Secretariat, losing control over meeting minutes and action items.

### Single-Point-of-Contact Discipline
The PM demands a single point of contact on the client side for each stream of input — clinical, commercial, FFE, IT. Multiple voices into the consultant team is the root cause of design churn. Where the client side is multi-organisation (co-developers, JV partners, agency + end-user), the single-point discipline is brokered into the SDP.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All projects with multiple client-side inputs.
Common pitfalls:
- Allowing user-group members to direct consultants outside of the formal consultation forum.
- Letting tenants or end-users issue technical directions to the contractor's site team.

### Drafting Around Controlling Commercial Agreements
Where the project sits within a controlling commercial agreement (Development Agreement, Partition Agreement, Stratum Management Agreement, JV deed), the PM is briefed on its terms before drafting the SDP — so the PDP does not create breach exposure. The PM's procurement strategy then aligns with the rights and obligations created by those agreements.

Reference: Industry practice; legal-led briefings.
Applies to: All co-developer, JV, stratum and similar structures.
Common pitfalls:
- Drafting an SDP without sight of the controlling agreement.
- Recommending procurement actions that contradict the agreement's mechanisms.

### Strategic vs Technical Interface Delineation
Strategic interface between co-developers / JV partners is the client's role. The PM supports technical and commercial interface — design meetings, RFI process, change control, utility and authority meetings. Behavioural framing recognises that successful joint delivery requires a "best for project" culture that, while considering respective objectives, navigates transparent and honest consideration when there is a lack of alignment.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: Multi-party projects with co-developer or co-funder relationships.
Common pitfalls:
- PM straying into strategic interface, creating tension with the client lead.
- Allowing technical interface to drift up to ESC level, slowing decision-making.

## 7. Time, Scope, Cost, Quality, Risk — Five Live Registers

### Time Management and the Agreed Programme
An Agreed Programme is established at the outset, derived from the client's reference programme. The PM statuses the programme monthly. A specialist in-house construction planner (or sub-consulted scheduler) reviews the critical path, scheduling assumptions, and the contractor's construction methodology programmes (tender and post-contract). Re-sequencing and mitigation strategies are offered, not imposed.

Reference: PMBOK; AS ISO 21500.
Applies to: All commissions with formal programme deliverables.
Common pitfalls:
- Statusing the programme as percent-complete only, missing leading indicators of slippage.
- Failing to obtain a contractor-prepared, baselined construction programme post-award.

### Scope Control via Change Control Registers
Project objectives, quality expectations, design intent, and feasibility assumptions are consolidated and used as the control for change. For residential: an apartment-mix Schedule of Accommodation and locked interior fixtures and finishes to inform display-suite and sales-contract collateral. For commercial: NLA-control Schedule of Accommodation; Tenancy Fitout Guide drafted with the client.

A Change Control Register is maintained for each stratum / scope separately.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All projects with sales-driven or NLA-driven feasibility assumptions.
Common pitfalls:
- Combining residential and commercial change registers, losing visibility per stratum.
- Allowing change to interior fixtures and finishes after display-suite sales begin.

### Cost Management Architecture
An Independent QS / Cost Manager is appointed (co-funded where applicable). The PM does cost reporting, budget reconciliation, contingency advice, consultant payments, and contractor progress claim certification. Variations above a threshold (typically ~$10,000) escalate to the independent QS; alternatively the PM's in-house QS supports variation assessment.

Cost flags at concept stage — particularly where a call on executive contingency may become necessary — are signalled pre-emptively.

Reference: AIQS guidelines; industry practice.
Applies to: All projects with formal cost management.
Common pitfalls:
- Certifying progress claims without QS reconciliation.
- Surprising the sponsor with a contingency call at schematic when concept-stage indicators existed.

### Quality Plans and Prototyping Discipline
The Project Quality Plan is the umbrella; consultant and contractor organisational quality plans nest beneath. A display suite is procured as a separate early-works package built off-site for residential / sales-driven projects. Prototype rooms — kitchens, bathrooms, key clinical rooms — are built early or off-site for coordination, detailing, and interface validation.

The construction-phase Quality Plan addresses witness points, inspection points, samples, prototypes, and submissions. Where consultants are novated to the D&C contractor, a peer-review consultant team is retained.

Reference: ISO 9001; AS/NZS ISO 9001:2016; industry practice.
Applies to: All quality-sensitive projects (luxury residential, healthcare, education).
Common pitfalls:
- Not commissioning a display suite or prototype rooms, then defecting the entire works during fitout.
- Novating consultants without retaining peer review.

### Structured Risk Register Format
A formal Risk Strategy uses a structured format: Risk → Cause → Consequence / Likelihood → Mitigating Action. Reporting forums are named: ESC monthly with high-rated risks summary; PCG monthly with full register as appendix; executive risk portal quarterly. The cost manager prepares a supplementary cost report addressing financial risks.

Reference: AS/NZS ISO 31000:2018 Risk Management; industry practice.
Applies to: All projects with formal risk reporting.
Common pitfalls:
- Listing risks without causes or mitigations, producing a record but not a tool.
- Failing to escalate cost-implication risks to the executive forum.

## 8. Resourcing and Fee Composition

### Programme-Linked Fee Derivation
The PM bids back the indicative programme as the basis for fee derivation — stating explicitly that the fee is conditional on programme stability and reserving the right to revisit if the programme moves materially. A resource schedule is provided as an appendix showing monthly percentage allocation per named person, summed to FTE per month and total hours over the commission.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All competitive PM fee proposals.
Common pitfalls:
- Fee offered without a referenced programme, making any drift a contractual flashpoint.
- Resource schedule that does not reconcile to the headline fee.

### The Leveraged Team Pyramid
A leveraged team pyramid is sized to phase intensity: Project Director (oversight, governance, legal interface), one or two Senior PMs (day-to-day project lead), PM (day-to-day support, on-site presence), Assistant / Cadet PM (QA/QC, defecting, support). The pyramid is sized higher in pre-contract phases, weighted to delivery roles in construction, and tapered in DLP.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All client-side PM commissions of meaningful scale.
Common pitfalls:
- Front-loading senior resource and under-resourcing the construction phase.
- Cadet/assistant resource missing from defecting and DLP planning.

### Specialist Heads as Available Adjuncts
Specialist heads — planning & programming, commercial / claims, in-house QS — are available on request but excluded from the base fee. Sister-discipline expertise is referenced in the methodology so the client understands which problem it would be called on to solve. Where the firm is single-discipline, the equivalent move is to name the specialist sub-consultants the PM will procure.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All proposals where specialist scope is foreseeable but not appointed.
Common pitfalls:
- Including specialist scope (claims defence, in-depth scheduling) silently in the base fee.
- Not naming the specialists the PM would call on, leaving the client uncertain about depth.

### Hourly Rates and Indexation
Hourly rates per role are published for variations — typically with a stated annual indexation rule (e.g. 3% at 30 June compounding, with cadet rates indexed higher to track entry-level market growth). Disbursements default to nil where attendance is assumed all on one site; flagged with the qualifying assumption.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All competitive PM fee proposals.
Common pitfalls:
- Failing to declare the indexation rule, then triggering disputes at FY end.
- Disbursements presented as open-ended, signalling uncertainty.

### Phase-by-Phase Minimum Hours
For fixed-scope government tenders, the PM produces a phase-by-phase minimum-hour matrix per named person, with a totalled minimum-hours summary. This becomes the basis for variation entitlement when phase durations extend.

Reference: NSW Procurement Rules; HI tender returnable schedules; industry practice for government PM appointments.
Applies to: Public-sector PM commissions.
Common pitfalls:
- Submitting minimum hours that imply a leaner resource than is feasible.
- Treating minimum hours as a target rather than a floor.

## 9. Key Personnel Positioning

### Direct, Current, Sector-Specific Experience
Each personnel paragraph in a strong proposal is engineered to prove three things: direct, current, recent experience with the specific type of project (sector, scale, complexity, contracting form); a relevant credential or reference the evaluator can validate (named referee with phone number, prior project with comparable contractual structure such as GC21 PAP work for a GC21 tender, familiarity with the specific authority); and defined role on this commission — what they personally will do, what they will lead, what they will hand to whom.

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All proposals at the key-personnel section.
Common pitfalls:
- Citing project experience that is institutional rather than personal.
- Vague "support" roles instead of defined responsibilities.

### CV Content Standards
CVs include: career chronology with sector, project value, role title; specialisations list (focused 6–10 bullets) phrased as judgement calls the person can make rather than generic activities; industry sectors served; formal qualifications and contract / standards literacy (e.g. GC21 Edition 2, Section J energy reporting, ISO 9001).

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All key-personnel CV appendices.
Common pitfalls:
- Boilerplate specialisation lists indistinguishable across staff.
- Omitting contract-form literacy (e.g. claiming GC21 experience without naming the prior GC21 projects).

## 10. Capability, Capacity, Continuity

### Three Distinct Claims
Capability (can the team do it — refer back to CVs), capacity (are the people available now in the volumes required — demonstrated by a current-commitments table listing each key person, their current projects, percentage of time committed, and expected completion dates), and continuity / redundancy (short-term alternate within the team and medium-term alternate deeper in the business, with the qualifier that this is not an intention to substitute but a continuity plan).

Reference: Industry practice.
Applies to: All competitive PM appointments.
Common pitfalls:
- Conflating capability and capacity in a single claim.
- Omitting current commitments, signalling either inattention or capacity stress.

## 11. Commercial Position and Risk Allocation

### Payment Terms and Invoicing
Payment terms typically 28 days from monthly invoice; some fee schedules invoice fortnightly. Interest on overdue payments — commonly 2% over the prevailing RBA cash rate — is reserved.

Reference: Industry standard short-form consultancy agreements.
Applies to: All PM consultancy agreements.
Common pitfalls:
- Accepting 60+ day terms without a fee adjustment for working capital cost.
- Omitting an interest clause, leaving the firm exposed on slow payments.

### Limitation of Liability and Consequential Loss
Liability is capped at a percentage of fees actually paid (50%, or 100% of contract sum in higher-risk roles), except where liability cannot be excluded by law. Backed by a worked example clause in an appendix. A separate clause excludes indirect / consequential losses (loss of use, data, profit, revenue, business, anticipated saving, goodwill, reputation, financing costs).

Reference: Standard consultancy agreement conventions; PI insurance market practice.
Applies to: All consultancy agreements.
Common pitfalls:
- Accepting uncapped liability for a sub-1% fee commission.
- Allowing a consequential loss clause that does not align with PI cover.

### Proportionate Liability
The PM typically declines to contract out of proportionate-liability legislation because PI insurance does not support it. Where the client mandates contracting out, a fee uplift is quoted reflecting the additional PI cover.

Reference: Civil Liability Act (NSW) Part 4; Wrongs Act (VIC) Part IVAA; equivalent state legislation.
Applies to: All PM consultancy agreements in Australia.
Common pitfalls:
- Signing a contracting-out clause without informing the PI insurer.
- Not quoting a fee uplift when the client insists.

### Professional Indemnity Insurance Settings
A stated PI cover (commonly $10m with one reinstatement) and a parallel quote for a higher cover ($20m with reinstatement) if mandated, with a fee uplift estimate (typically $30k–$50k per annum of additional premium for $10m → $20m step-up on a mid-sized firm).

Reference: PI insurance broker advice; industry practice.
Applies to: All consultancy proposals with PI requirements.
Common pitfalls:
- Quoting PI uplift without confirming with the broker first.
- PI cover stated without reinstatement provision.

### EOT and Fee Variation Entitlements
EOT entitlement is sought for events outside the PM's reasonable control — including contractor actions affecting the PM's scope. A corresponding fee variation is sought where the intensity of services genuinely changes, not at the sole discretion of the Principal. Variation rate basis is disclosed (hourly rates per role).

Reference: Standard consultancy agreement conventions.
Applies to: All PM consultancy agreements.
Common pitfalls:
- Accepting EOT only at Principal discretion.
- Failing to define what triggers a fee variation (e.g. scope increase, programme intensification, scope outside control).

### Disbursements, IP and Non-Solicitation
Disbursements typically actual cost plus 10% handling. IP: PM retains copyright; client granted a non-transferable royalty-free licence for the Commission upon fee payment. Non-solicitation: staff non-poach clause with liquidated damages (typically 25% of remuneration package) for breach within 6 months of completion.

Reference: Standard consultancy agreement conventions.
Applies to: All PM consultancy agreements.
Common pitfalls:
- Disbursement markup omitted, leaving the firm subsidising sub-consultants' travel.
- IP fully transferred, preventing the firm from reusing methodology assets.

### Dispute Resolution
Staged dispute resolution: written notice → confer within 5 business days → 28-day window for negotiated resolution → UNCITRAL arbitration (or jurisdiction per schedule), English language, one arbitrator. Continuing performance during dispute is required.

Reference: UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules; standard consultancy agreement conventions.
Applies to: All PM consultancy agreements.
Common pitfalls:
- Litigation default without a negotiated step.
- Allowing performance to suspend during dispute, exposing the client to programme delay.

## 12. Recurring Project Risk Themes

### Ground Plane, Basement, and Flood Risk
Interaction of basement excavation, on-site detention, overland flood paths, regional stormwater upgrades, and authority risk-aversion around approving flood-affected developments is often the single most budget-consuming issue on a constrained urban site. Early engagement of a flood consultant and civil engineer, with flood modelling conducted before concept design endorsement, is the standard mitigation.

Reference: AS 3500 hydraulics; relevant state flood policy (e.g. NSW Floodplain Development Manual); local DCP stormwater requirements.
Applies to: Urban infill projects in flood-prone precincts.
Common pitfalls:
- Concept design endorsed before flood modelling.
- Basement design proceeding without ground plane / threshold RL resolution.

### Contamination and UST Removal
Particularly on former service station or commercial sites: site auditor advice, interim audit advice for subdivision, environmental clearance sequencing. The PM works with the Early Works PM (where contamination removal is in a separate package) to understand remaining issues confronting the Main Works and address early to gain cost confidence on mitigation.

Reference: NSW POEO Act 1997; POEO (Underground Petroleum Storage Systems) Regulation 2019; equivalent state legislation; AS 4482 (Contaminated Sites Guide); NEPM (National Environment Protection Measure).
Applies to: Brownfield and adaptive-reuse projects.
Common pitfalls:
- Discovering residual contamination in basement excavation without budget allocation.
- Subdivision certificate delays caused by missing audit advice.

### Heritage Constraints
Facade retention, false-work, localised strengthening, preservation of identified fabric, two-stage DA with design competition, design excellence pathways. Heritage adaptive-reuse projects typically involve significant cost transfer of strengthening and heritage preservation works to the developer of any addition (e.g. residential tower atop heritage retail base).

Reference: NSW Heritage Act 1977; Australia ICOMOS Burra Charter; local heritage DCPs.
Applies to: Heritage and adaptive-reuse projects.
Common pitfalls:
- Underestimating the structural and temporary works cost of heritage retention.
- Assuming heritage approval will accept generic facade interventions.

### Site Access and Materials Handling
Crane locations, loading zones, air rights, structural strengthening to permit hoists, materials lift across adjacent properties, tower-crane repositioning between phases. CBD and constrained urban sites typically have logistics costs that rival the actual works cost.

Reference: WHS regulations; local council construction logistics requirements; industry practice.
Applies to: CBD, mid-rise and tight urban infill projects.
Common pitfalls:
- Crane and hoist strategy designed without input from likely tenderers.
- Loading zone permits assumed available without council confirmation.

### Adjoining Owner Negotiation
Boundary condition resolution, party-wall consultation, dilapidation surveys, and the limits of cooperation available from a neighbour competing for the same trades market. Early engagement of adjoining owners is the rule, particularly where temporary works rely on adjoining infrastructure.

Reference: Conveyancing Act / Real Property Acts in each jurisdiction; common law of nuisance; local council development requirements.
Applies to: All projects with shared boundary conditions.
Common pitfalls:
- Assuming an adjoining owner will cooperate with crane oversail or hoarding installation.
- Dilapidation surveys conducted post-incident rather than pre-commencement.

### Long-Lead Procurement Items
Structural steel (typically 12–16 weeks), glazing and facade (often 16–24+ weeks), specialist equipment (medical, dental, vertical transportation, kitchen). Lead-times can force the overall stage duration; the PM identifies these in the tender programme and tracks them as discrete milestones through trade procurement.

Reference: Industry practice; specialist trade lead-time guidance.
Applies to: All projects with non-commodity manufactured elements.
Common pitfalls:
- Trade procurement initiated post-mobilisation rather than pre-tender.
- Lead-times not surfaced in the construction programme until post-award.

### Tenancy and Operational Disruption
Staging negotiated with tenants; shutdown windows in the PPR; coordination with trading peaks (Spring, Christmas, academic year, financial-year close). For operating sites, the PM brokers the operational interface as a contract obligation rather than goodwill.

Reference: Industry practice; tenant lease provisions; agency operational plans.
Applies to: Refurbishment and upgrade works on operating sites.
Common pitfalls:
- PPR staging silent on trading peaks, leading to contractor pushback post-award.
- Tenant shutdown windows agreed verbally and not documented.

### Authority and Consent Risk
DA assessment duration is often unknown; conditions imposed late may not align with the forecast consent matrix used at tender; clause 4.6 / merit-assessment pathways may be needed for height / FSR exceedance. The PM plans contingency for both consent timing and consent-condition variance.

Reference: NSW EP&A Act 1979; SEPP suite; local DCPs.
Applies to: All projects requiring DA or equivalent state planning consent.
Common pitfalls:
- Tender award timed to a "best case" consent date.
- No reserve in the programme for technical-input cycles with the assessing authority.

### Market Shocks and Supply Chain Volatility
Pandemic, conflict, materials shortage, FX shifts — addressed by benchmarking tender returns through peer-review processes (HI ERG-equivalent), adjusting design or scope early when cost plans reveal market drift, and protecting against escalation in long-duration contracts via rise-and-fall provisions where appropriate.

Reference: Industry practice; commodity price indices.
Applies to: All projects exposed to global supply chains or volatile labour markets.
Common pitfalls:
- Locking lump sums without escalation provisions in inflationary periods.
- Cost planning that assumes static rates over multi-year programmes.

## 13. Authority and Stakeholder Mapping

### Statutory Referral Agencies
Water authority, energy distributor, gas distributor, telco / NBN, transport agency, rail-adjacent works approvals, heritage / Aboriginal heritage, environmental regulator. The PM maintains a stakeholder map and sequences consultations relative to design milestones — particularly authority pre-applications.

Reference: Relevant state planning legislation; agency-specific approval frameworks.
Applies to: All projects with utility or referral-agency dependencies.
Common pitfalls:
- HV supply or substation approval initiated too late, becoming a critical-path constraint.
- Aboriginal heritage assessment initiated post-DA, triggering re-lodgement.

### Public-Sector Sponsor Layers
For public-sector commissions, the governance layer includes program steering committee, sponsoring ministry / department, end-user agency, treasury / cost-plan approver. The PM acts as Secretariat to one or more layers and supports delegate-level approvals through the formal portal / governance system (e.g. HI Portal).

Reference: NSW Treasury TPP15-03 (Major Project Assurance); equivalent state frameworks; HI governance framework.
Applies to: Public-sector PM commissions.
Common pitfalls:
- Project brief submitted to sponsor without sequenced engagement of treasury or asset agency.
- Delegate approval timing not built into programme.

## 14. Proposal Document Architecture

### Private-Client RFP Section Order
Cover letter restating the opportunity; executive summary / value proposition; conflicts of interest disclosure; returnable schedules (fee, key personnel, hourly rates); procurement and delivery strategy (the strategic core); phase-by-phase methodology; risk, time, scope, cost, quality management approaches; past projects with named references and contractual structure; company QA / management systems; current commissions / capacity / continuity; mark-up of the client's draft PM Agreement clauses with commercial departures grouped; appendices (programme, resource schedule, CVs, accreditation, insurance certificates, example clauses).

Reference: Industry practice for client-side PM bids.
Applies to: All competitive private-client RFP responses.
Common pitfalls:
- Burying the procurement strategy mid-document; it is the strategic core and belongs near the front.
- Commercial departures presented as separate emails rather than a clause-by-clause mark-up in the response.

### Government Tender Section Order
Tender form (deed-poll execution); lump sum fee + breakdown by program part (e.g. HI Parts 0–9); rates for variations; non-compliances and qualifications; project plan — understanding, methodology, communication, coordination, tender programme; experience of key personnel (with maximum-page limits); organisational experience — sample similar commissions with named client referees; risk management strategy (table format); resource schedule (minimum hours per phase, per person); management systems (QMS, EMS, OHSMS) with accreditation certificates; insurance certificates of currency; CVs as attachments.

Reference: NSW Government tender templates; HI Capital and Service Consultants tender schedules; equivalent state frameworks.
Applies to: Government tender responses.
Common pitfalls:
- Exceeding page limits, risking non-conformance.
- Missing or out-of-date insurance certificates of currency.
- Risk strategy presented as prose rather than the mandated table format.

### Mandatory Appendices
Indicative programme; minimum resource schedule per phase per person; CVs of all key personnel; QMS / EMS / OHSMS certifications (or status statement if not certified); PI / public liability / workers comp insurance certificates of currency; example clauses for limitation of liability, indirect loss, professional indemnity reinstatement; current commitments schedule for each key person; conflict of interest declaration.

Reference: Industry practice; tender-specific returnable schedules.
Applies to: All competitive PM bids.
Common pitfalls:
- Insurance certificates issued by the broker rather than the insurer (some tenders specify insurer-issued only).
- Current commitments schedule not reconciled to the resource schedule.
