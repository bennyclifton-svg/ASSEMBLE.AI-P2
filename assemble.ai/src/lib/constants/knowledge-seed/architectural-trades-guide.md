# Architectural Trades Guide

## Facade Systems — Curtain Wall, Panel, and Brick Veneer Selection Criteria

The facade is the single most expensive architectural trade package on most multi-storey projects and sits on the critical path for every internal finish that follows. Selecting the wrong facade system — or selecting the right system and procuring it too late — is one of the most consequential errors a PM can make. The choice between unitised curtain wall, stick curtain wall, cladding panel systems, and traditional brick veneer is driven by building height, program constraints, thermal performance requirements under NCC 2024 Section J, and the project's risk appetite for weather-dependent site installation.

Unitised curtain wall systems are factory-assembled panels shipped to site as completed units, craned into position and clipped to facade brackets. They are the preferred solution for buildings above 15-20 storeys because installation speed is high (4-6 panels per hour with an experienced crew), quality is factory-controlled, and site labour is minimised. However, unitised systems carry the longest lead time of any architectural trade item at 20-30 weeks from design commencement to first delivery (6-8 weeks design and shop drawings, 12-16 weeks fabrication, 2-6 weeks shipping for imported systems). The cost range is $800-$2,500/m2 installed depending on complexity, performance specification, and aluminium market pricing. Any design change after fabrication commencement will cost $50,000-$500,000+ in rework and reset the fabrication schedule — this must be communicated forcefully to the design team and client at the point of design freeze.

Stick curtain wall systems are assembled on site from individual mullions, transoms, and glazing units. They are typically used on buildings of 4-15 storeys where the facade area does not justify the tooling cost of a unitised system. Lead times are shorter at 14-20 weeks, and installed cost is $600-$1,500/m2, but installation is slower, more weather-sensitive, and requires scaffolding or mast climbers. Stick systems are more forgiving of late design changes than unitised, but any change after mullion extrusions are ordered still carries significant cost and delay.

Post-Grenfell and post-Lacrosse, every facade material selection must be assessed against NCC 2024 Volume 1 Part C (fire safety) and the applicable state combustible cladding regulations. The estimated national rectification liability for combustible cladding exceeds $6 billion. Every state has established audit and rectification programs, but the specific requirements and thresholds differ by jurisdiction. NSW (Fair Trading), VIC (Cladding Safety Victoria), and QLD (QBCC) have the most mature programs. A PM who selects a facade material without confirming its compliance with both the NCC and the applicable state combustible cladding provisions is accepting an unquantifiable risk.

NCC 2024 Section J energy efficiency requirements have made double glazing effectively mandatory in climate zones 2-8, adding $80-$150/m2 to glazing costs over single glazing. Facade U-values and solar heat gain coefficients (SHGC) now significantly influence facade system selection and cost. This is frequently discovered late in design development, resetting facade procurement timelines.

Reference: NCC 2024 Volume 1 Part C (fire), Part F6 (energy), Section J; AS 2047-2014 (windows); AS 1288-2006 (glass); AS 4284-2008 (testing of building facades)
Applies to: new, refurb — commercial, residential, mixed-use multi-storey
Related standards: AS 1530.1-1530.4 (fire testing); state combustible cladding regulations (NSW, VIC, QLD, WA, SA)

Common pitfalls:
- Failing to lock facade design before unitised curtain wall fabrication begins — a single design change after fabrication start costs $50,000-$500,000+ and resets the programme by 8-16 weeks
- Not coordinating facade bracket design with the structural engineer's as-built tolerance survey — facade brackets must accommodate real slab-edge deviations, not theoretical dimensions
- Selecting facade materials without checking both NCC 2024 combustible cladding provisions AND the relevant state cladding audit program requirements, which differ by jurisdiction
- Underestimating the cost impact of NCC 2024 Section J energy requirements discovered late in design — double glazing and improved U-values add $80-$150/m2 and can reset procurement timelines

## Glazing and Window Systems — Performance Specifications and Procurement

Windows and glazing are on the critical path for building weathertightness, which is the single most important programme milestone for enabling internal finishes. A one-week delay in window installation cascades through waterproofing, linings, joinery, painting, and floor finishes — the multiplier effect means that a single week of window delay often translates into 0.8 weeks of handover delay after accounting for limited overlap opportunities.

Aluminium windows for residential projects carry a lead time of 10-16 weeks (2-4 weeks design, 6-10 weeks fabrication, 2 weeks delivery), with installed costs ranging from $400-$1,200/m2 depending on performance rating and glazing specification. The Australian Window Association (AWA) certification scheme adds 2-4 weeks to procurement if the specified product is not already pre-certified — PMs should verify AWA certification status during tender evaluation, not after contract award.

NCC 2024 energy efficiency requirements have fundamentally changed glazing specifications for most of Australia. Double glazing is now effectively mandatory in climate zones 2-8, which covers all major metropolitan areas except parts of tropical Queensland. The cost differential between single and double glazing is $80-$150/m2. This requirement is frequently missed or underspecified during early design, and its discovery during design development or construction documentation resets the glazing procurement timeline. PMs should flag glazing specification compliance with NCC Section J as a design coordination risk at the earliest project stage.

Performance specifications for windows must address water penetration resistance (tested to AS 2047), structural wind load resistance, air infiltration, acoustic performance (Rw rating), and thermal performance (U-value and SHGC). In cyclone regions (north of the cyclone line, primarily in QLD, WA, and NT), windows must also meet specific debris-impact and wind-pressure ratings per AS 1170.2 and relevant state planning instruments. Cyclone-rated windows carry a 15-30% cost premium and 2-4 weeks additional lead time over standard products.

Glazing selection under AS 1288 requires consideration of human impact safety (Grade A safety glass in locations as defined by AS 1288), wind load, thermal stress, and where relevant, bushfire resistance (AS 3959 BAL ratings for bushfire-prone areas). Toughened glass, laminated glass, and insulated glass units (IGUs) each have different performance characteristics, lead times, and cost implications. Large-format IGUs for commercial curtain wall systems are typically imported and carry 12-20 week lead times.

Reference: AS 2047-2014 (windows, performance requirements); AS 1288-2006 (glass in buildings); NCC 2024 Part F6 and Section J; AS 1170.2 (wind actions); AWA performance certification scheme
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes
Related standards: AS 3959-2018 (bushfire); AS 4055-2012 (wind classification for housing); AS 2208 (safety glazing for buildings)

Common pitfalls:
- Not verifying AWA certification status during tender evaluation, discovering 2-4 weeks additional procurement time after contract award
- Failing to coordinate glazing specification with NCC 2024 Section J energy requirements during early design, resulting in a specification upgrade and procurement reset during construction
- Specifying window performance without considering the full envelope — a high-performance window in a poorly sealed wall assembly still fails the system air-tightness test
- Ordering glazing quantities without adequate wastage allowance (typically 3-5%) — breakage during installation and handling is inevitable, and replacement glass from a different batch may not colour-match

## External Waterproofing — Membrane Systems, Application, and Warranty Structures

External waterproofing is the building envelope's last line of defence against water ingress, and its failure is the single most expensive facade-related defect to remediate because of the access costs (scaffold or rope access), the scale of affected area, and the consequential damage to internal finishes, structure, and building services. Across all Australian state building authorities, waterproofing defects consistently represent the single largest complaint category — QBCC data shows waterproofing and wet area defects account for 25-35% of all building defect complaints. The remediation-to-prevention cost ratio for waterproofing is approximately 20:1. Every $1,000 invested in proper waterproofing inspection, testing, and applicator selection prevents $20,000 or more in remediation costs.

External waterproofing membranes fall into two primary categories: torch-on modified bitumen membranes and liquid-applied membranes. Torch-on modified bitumen (such as Bitumode, Evalon, or Wolfin sheet membranes) is the traditional choice for horizontal surfaces — roofs, podium decks, planter boxes, and balconies subjected to ponding water or hydrostatic pressure. It provides a thick, durable, and UV-stable membrane that can accommodate structural movement when properly detailed. Application requires a qualified applicator with hot-work permits, and the work is weather-sensitive — ambient temperature must be above 5 degrees Celsius, the substrate must be dry, and application during rain or high wind is not possible. Material costs are $15-$60/m2 for the membrane alone, with installed costs of $80-$200/m2 including substrate preparation, priming, and detailing.

Liquid-applied membranes (polyurethane, polyurea, acrylic, or cementitious) are increasingly used for vertical surfaces, complex geometries, and areas where torch-on application is impractical. They are spray-applied or roller-applied and form a seamless monolithic membrane. Cure times are temperature-dependent — 24-72 hours before the membrane is trafficable, longer in cold or humid conditions. The critical weakness of liquid membranes is that film thickness is applicator-dependent and difficult to verify visually. Wet-film thickness (WFT) checks during application and dry-film thickness (DFT) testing after curing are essential quality control measures that many PMs fail to specify or enforce.

The most common failure mode for external waterproofing is at junctions, penetrations, and changes of plane — the membrane field is rarely the problem. Upstand heights, termination details at door thresholds, pipe and conduit penetrations, and movement joints are where failures occur. AS 4654.2 specifies minimum upstand heights of 150mm above finished floor level for balconies and terraces, and details for penetrations and junctions. These details must be clearly shown on the waterproofing shop drawings, reviewed by the PM, and inspected at hold-point stage before any tiling, paving, or topping is applied.

Waterproofing is a mandatory hold-point trade. No subsequent work — no screeds, no tiling, no paving, no planting — should proceed over any waterproofed surface until the membrane has been inspected and, where applicable, flood-tested and approved. On external decks and podiums, flood testing is not always practical, but adhesion testing, DFT testing, and visual inspection of all details are non-negotiable.

Warranty structures for external waterproofing typically range from 10 to 25 years depending on membrane type and manufacturer. However, warranty conditions invariably require that application was performed by an approved or certified applicator, that substrate preparation met the manufacturer's requirements, and that the membrane was not damaged by subsequent trades. A PM who does not document compliance with warranty conditions during construction is handing the client a potentially worthless warranty.

Reference: AS 4654.1-2012 (waterproofing membranes for external above-ground use — requirements); AS 4654.2-2012 (design and installation); NCC 2024 Part F1 (damp and weatherproofing)
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes with external waterproofing requirements
Related standards: AS 3740-2021 (internal wet areas — cross-reference for threshold detailing); manufacturer technical data sheets and warranty conditions

Common pitfalls:
- Treating waterproofing as a commodity trade and selecting on price alone — the cost difference between a competent and incompetent applicator is trivial ($5-$15/m2) compared to the $200-$500/m2 remediation cost when the membrane fails
- Not specifying or enforcing WFT and DFT testing during application, resulting in insufficient membrane thickness discovered only when leaks appear years later
- Allowing subsequent trades to traffic or penetrate completed waterproofing before inspection and approval — a single penetration can compromise the entire membrane system
- Failing to document warranty condition compliance (applicator certification, substrate preparation, inspection records) during construction, leaving the client with an unenforceable warranty
- Not coordinating waterproofing upstand heights with door threshold and balustrade base-plate details, creating conflicts that are resolved on site by reducing upstand heights below the AS 4654.2 minimum

## Internal Waterproofing — Wet Area Membranes, Flood Testing, and Defect Prevention

Internal waterproofing of wet areas is the single highest-risk trade activity in Australian residential and multi-residential construction by volume of defect complaints. QBCC data consistently shows wet area waterproofing defects as the largest single category of building complaints, representing 25-35% of all defect complaints lodged with state building authorities. The remediation cost for a failed bathroom waterproofing membrane is disproportionate to the original application cost: a bathroom waterproofing application costs $50-$120/m2 ($800-$2,000 per bathroom), while remediation of a failed membrane requires demolition of tiles, removal of the compromised membrane, re-waterproofing, re-tiling, and repair of water damage to adjacent areas — typically $15,000-$40,000 per bathroom, and significantly more if structural timber framing or adjacent dwellings are affected.

AS 3740-2021 (Waterproofing of domestic wet areas) is the governing standard and is referenced by NCC 2024 Volume 2 for Class 1 and Class 10 buildings, and NCC Volume 1 for Class 2-9 buildings. The standard specifies membrane performance requirements, minimum application areas (all shower recesses, bath areas, laundries, and the floor of any room containing a sanitary fixture), junction and penetration detailing, and testing requirements. Critically, AS 3740 requires that the waterproofing membrane extend a minimum of 150mm above the finished floor level at junctions with walls, 25mm above the hob of a shower recess (where a hob exists), and to the full height of walls in shower recesses (1800mm minimum above finished floor level, or to ceiling height in frameless shower configurations).

The application sequence for internal wet area waterproofing is: (1) substrate preparation — the concrete or fibre-cement substrate must be clean, dry, and free of contaminants, with all penetrations (waste pipes, water supply, floor drains) installed and secured; (2) priming — the substrate is primed with the membrane manufacturer's specified primer; (3) first coat — the membrane is applied, typically by roller or brush, embedding a reinforcing fabric (bandage) at all internal corners, junctions, and penetrations; (4) second coat — applied perpendicular to the first coat after the first coat has cured (typically 4-12 hours depending on product and temperature); (5) curing — the membrane must cure fully before any subsequent work (24-48 hours minimum); (6) flood testing — the completed membrane is flood-tested by filling the wet area with water to a depth of 25mm above the highest point of the membrane (or above the hob for shower recesses) and holding for a minimum of 24 hours; (7) inspection — the membrane and flood test result are inspected and approved before tiling can commence.

Flood testing is a mandatory hold point. In Queensland, flood testing is a QBCC regulatory requirement and failure to flood test is a compliance breach. Other states are increasingly adopting flood testing as standard practice, and AS 3740-2021 requires it. A project with 200 bathrooms testing at 10 per day requires 4 weeks of flood testing programme — this must be sequenced with the waterproofing crew's production rate and the overall internal finishes programme. PMs who do not programme flood testing as a discrete activity with adequate duration invariably create a bottleneck that delays tiling, joinery, painting, and handover.

The most common failure modes for internal waterproofing are: (1) inadequate substrate preparation — residual dust, laitance, or moisture preventing membrane adhesion; (2) insufficient membrane thickness — too few coats or coats applied too thin; (3) poor junction detailing — failure to embed reinforcing fabric at internal corners and penetrations; (4) mechanical damage by subsequent trades — plumbers, electricians, or tilers penetrating the cured membrane to install or adjust fittings; and (5) insufficient cure time — tiling applied before the membrane has fully cured, trapping moisture and preventing adhesion. Each of these failure modes is preventable through proper specification, applicator selection, and hold-point inspection.

The coordination risk between waterproofing and plumbing is acute. All plumbing penetrations through the waterproofing membrane — waste outlets, water supply risers, floor drains — must be installed before waterproofing commences. A plumber who returns to a waterproofed bathroom to move a waste outlet or add a penetration will breach the membrane, requiring re-waterproofing of the affected area at a cost of $2,000-$5,000 per bathroom. The PM must enforce a hard coordination rule: no plumbing changes after waterproofing is complete.

Reference: AS 3740-2021 (waterproofing of domestic wet areas); NCC 2024 Volume 1 Part F1 and Volume 2 Part 3.8.1; QBCC waterproofing inspection guidelines; AIW (Australian Institute of Waterproofing) best practice guides
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes with wet areas
Related standards: AS 4654.2-2012 (external waterproofing — cross-reference for threshold and balcony details); AS 3958.1-2007 (ceramic tile installation — interface with waterproofing)

Common pitfalls:
- Not programming flood testing as a discrete activity — 200 bathrooms at 10 per day = 4 weeks of testing, which must be sequenced with waterproofing production rate and tiling commencement
- Allowing plumbing changes after waterproofing is complete — re-waterproofing costs $2,000-$5,000 per bathroom and delays the unit by 5-7 days for re-application, cure, and re-testing
- Selecting waterproofing applicators on price alone — a $200-$400 saving per bathroom on application cost versus a $15,000-$40,000 remediation cost if the membrane fails
- Not enforcing the hold point — once tiles are installed over a defective membrane, the only remedy is full demolition and re-waterproofing ($15,000-$40,000 per bathroom)
- Failing to check membrane manufacturer's compatibility with proposed tile adhesive — incompatible products can cause delamination that does not manifest until months after handover

## Masonry — Brick and Blockwork, Cavity Construction, and Fire-Rated Walls

Masonry construction in Australia is governed by AS 3700-2018 (Masonry structures) and is used extensively in residential construction (brick veneer), commercial construction (concrete blockwork for fire-rated and structural walls), and mixed-use developments (both brick veneer cladding and loadbearing blockwork). The PM's primary concerns with masonry are cavity wall drainage and ventilation, fire-rating compliance, control joint placement, and the interface between masonry and other building elements — particularly at window and door openings, floor junctions, and roof lines.

Brick veneer construction remains the dominant external wall system for Class 1 buildings (houses) and is common for Class 2 buildings (apartments) up to about 8-10 storeys. The cavity between the brick veneer skin and the structural frame (timber or steel) must include a minimum 40mm clear cavity (NCC 2024 Volume 2), a reflective sarking or building wrap that acts as both a weather barrier and thermal reflective layer, and weep holes at the base of the cavity (at maximum 1200mm centres) to allow drainage of any moisture that penetrates the brick skin. The base of the cavity must be kept clear of mortar droppings, which obstruct drainage and create moisture bridges — a defect that is invisible once construction is complete and manifests as damp patches on internal linings. Cavity cleanliness should be verified by the PM at the hold-point inspection before the cavity is closed.

Concrete blockwork walls used for fire compartmentation must comply with the specific fire-resistance level (FRL) required by NCC 2024 Volume 1 Part C. An FRL is expressed as three values — structural adequacy / integrity / insulation — in minutes (for example, 120/120/120 for a 2-hour fire-rated wall). The FRL is achieved through a combination of block type (hollow or solid), block width (typically 140mm, 190mm, or 290mm), mortar type, and any applied fire-rated coating. A 190mm concrete blockwork wall with cores filled with grout and reinforcement typically achieves an FRL of 120/120/120. The critical PM risk is that fire-rated blockwork walls must extend from slab to slab (or slab to underside of slab above) without gaps — any gap at the top of the wall, or any ungrouted cores in fire-rated sections, compromises the fire rating. This is a frequent inspection failure point.

Control joints in masonry must be placed at maximum centres specified by AS 3700 (typically 6-8m for clay brickwork, 4-6m for concrete blockwork) and at locations of stress concentration — adjacent to openings, at changes in wall height or thickness, and at building movement joints. Missing or incorrectly placed control joints result in cracking, which is the most visible and common masonry defect. While cracking is often non-structural, its remediation is expensive (re-pointing or crack injection at $50-$150 per linear metre) and affects building aesthetics and weathertightness.

Reference: AS 3700-2018 (masonry structures); NCC 2024 Part C (fire compartmentation), Part F1 (damp and weatherproofing); AS 4773.1-2015 and AS 4773.2-2015 (masonry in small buildings)
Applies to: new, extend — residential (Class 1, 2) and commercial (Class 5-9)
Related standards: AS 1530.4 (fire resistance testing); AS 2699 (wall ties); AS 3600-2018 (concrete structures — for grouted blockwork)

Common pitfalls:
- Mortar droppings bridging the cavity in brick veneer walls, creating moisture paths to internal linings that manifest as damp patches months after handover — verify cavity cleanliness at hold-point inspection
- Fire-rated blockwork walls not extending fully from slab to slab, or having ungrouted cores in fire-rated sections, compromising the fire compartment and creating a compliance failure
- Omitting or incorrectly spacing control joints, resulting in visible cracking that requires re-pointing at $50-$150/lm
- Not coordinating weep hole locations with finished ground levels, landscaping, or paving — blocked weep holes prevent cavity drainage and cause damp

## Internal Partitions and Linings — Plasterboard, Fire-Rating, and Acoustic Performance

Internal partitions and linings are the backbone of the internal fit-out and directly affect fire compartmentation, acoustic separation, and the quality of finished surfaces. The Association of Wall and Ceiling Industries (AWCI) publishes fire-rated and acoustic-rated system specifications that must be followed exactly — site substitution of materials, changes to stud spacing, or omission of resilient mounts invalidates the system rating and creates a compliance failure that is invisible once linings are closed up.

Standard plasterboard linings cost $12-$25/m2 for supply and are used for general partitions and ceiling linings. Fire-rated systems range from $35-$80/m2 installed, depending on the FRL required and the complexity of the system (single layer, double layer, shaft wall, etc.). Acoustic systems incorporating resilient mounting channels, multiple plasterboard layers, and acoustic insulation range from $50-$120/m2 installed. Lead times for standard plasterboard are 2-4 weeks. Specialist fire-rated products such as Fyrchek and Fyrchek MR can have longer lead times of 4-8 weeks, particularly during periods of high market demand.

Fire-rated partition and lining systems must be installed strictly in accordance with the tested system specification. This means the exact stud type and gauge, stud spacing, number of plasterboard layers, plasterboard type (standard, fire-rated, moisture-resistant), fixing pattern (screw spacing and edge distances), and any specified insulation must match the tested system. A fire-rated system is only as good as its weakest component — a wall with the correct number of plasterboard layers but the wrong screw spacing does not achieve its rated FRL. The PM must ensure that the fire-rated system schedule is clearly documented, that the installing subcontractor has a copy of the relevant system specifications, and that hold-point inspections are conducted before second-side lining is installed (closing up the wall cavity and making inspection of insulation, services, and first-side fixing impossible).

NCC 2024 Part F5 significantly increased acoustic separation requirements for Class 2 buildings (apartments). Inter-tenancy walls and floors now require an impact-weighted sound reduction index (Rw + Ctr) of 50 or above. This is a step-change that has driven adoption of resilient-mounted lining systems, where the plasterboard is fixed to resilient channels or clips rather than directly to the studs. Resilient mounting breaks the vibration path through the framing, significantly improving acoustic performance but adding 1-2 weeks to installation programme per floor and increasing cost by $15-$30/m2. Acoustic flanking — sound transmission around, over, or under the partition rather than through it — is the most commonly missed compliance pathway. Common flanking paths include above the ceiling (through the ceiling plenum), under the floor (through the slab edge), at window mullion connections, and through back-to-back electrical outlets.

Reference: NCC 2024 Part C (fire), Part F5 (sound insulation); AS 1530.4 (fire resistance testing); AWCI fire-rated system specifications; AS/NZS 1276 series (acoustics)
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes
Related standards: AS 2588-2018 (gypsum plasterboard); AWCI Technical Notes

Common pitfalls:
- Site substitution of materials in fire-rated systems — using standard plasterboard instead of fire-rated, or changing stud gauge — invalidates the FRL and creates a hidden compliance failure
- Not conducting hold-point inspection before second-side lining closes up the wall cavity, making it impossible to verify insulation, services clearance, and first-side fixing
- Ignoring acoustic flanking paths — a partition that meets Rw 50 in isolation but flanks through the ceiling plenum or back-to-back power outlets will fail in-situ acoustic testing
- Installing resilient mounting systems with direct connections (hard points) that short-circuit the acoustic isolation — a single misplaced screw through a resilient channel to the stud defeats the entire system

## Ceiling Systems — Suspended Ceilings, Bulkheads, and MEP Coordination

Ceiling systems are one of the most coordination-intensive elements of the internal fit-out because they sit at the interface between architectural finishes and mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) services. The ceiling plane is shared by air conditioning diffusers, sprinkler heads, lighting, smoke detectors, speakers, access panels, and exhaust grilles — all of which must be coordinated in three dimensions before the ceiling grid is installed. A ceiling installed without complete services coordination above it will be penetrated, cut, and damaged by follow-on trades, resulting in patching, re-painting, and quality defects that are visible to building occupants.

Standard suspended mineral fibre ceiling systems (Armstrong, USG, Knauf) cost $40-$80/m2 installed and have lead times of 4-8 weeks. Metal panel ceilings cost $80-$200/m2, timber ceilings $120-$300/m2, and specialist acoustic ceilings can exceed $300/m2. Specialist ceiling types carry lead times of 8-14 weeks. The ceiling system must comply with AS/NZS 2785 for suspended ceiling installation, including seismic bracing requirements in relevant areas.

The fundamental rule for ceiling programme sequencing is: all above-ceiling services must be installed, tested, and inspected before the ceiling grid is installed. This includes HVAC ductwork and diffuser boots, fire sprinkler pipework (first fix), electrical conduit and cable trays, data cabling, plumbing waste and vent pipes in the ceiling zone, fire detection and alarm wiring, and smoke/fire dampers. If any of these services are incomplete when the ceiling is installed, the ceiling must be removed or penetrated to complete the work — at a cost of $20-$50/m2 for ceiling removal and reinstatement, plus programme delay.

The PM must enforce a clear hold point: no ceiling grid installation until all above-ceiling services are signed off as complete and inspected. This requires coordination with the MEP subcontractors to establish a floor-by-floor or zone-by-zone services completion programme that aligns with the ceiling installation programme. In practice, the services completion deadline must be set 1-2 weeks before the ceiling installation start date to provide float for remediation of any inspection defects.

Access panel locations must be coordinated with MEP maintenance requirements — fire dampers, valves, junction boxes, and other serviceable equipment above the ceiling must be accessible via appropriately sized and located access panels. Failure to coordinate access panels results in ceiling cutting during defect liability period maintenance, or in equipment that cannot be serviced, triggering occupant complaints and warranty claims.

Reference: AS/NZS 2785-2020 (suspended ceilings — design and installation); NCC 2024 Part C (fire separation requirements for ceiling membranes); NATSPEC ceiling specifications
Applies to: new, refurb — commercial (Class 5-9), multi-residential (Class 2)
Related standards: AS 1530.4 (fire testing for fire-rated ceiling membranes); AS 1668.1 (fire and smoke control)

Common pitfalls:
- Installing ceiling grid before above-ceiling services are complete and inspected — resulting in ceiling removal and reinstatement at $20-$50/m2 plus programme delay
- Not coordinating access panel locations with MEP maintenance requirements, leaving fire dampers, valves, and junction boxes inaccessible for ongoing maintenance
- Failing to programme the services completion deadline 1-2 weeks before ceiling installation to allow for inspection and remediation of defects above the ceiling
- Omitting seismic bracing for suspended ceilings in areas where AS/NZS 2785 requires it, creating a compliance failure and a safety risk in the event of seismic activity

## Floor Finishes — Tiles, Vinyl, Carpet, Timber, and Substrate Preparation

Floor finishes are the last architectural trade installed in most areas (to protect them from damage by other trades), which means they sit at the end of the critical path and have zero programme float — any delay to floor finishes delays handover directly. The PM's primary concerns are substrate preparation (flatness, moisture content, contamination), slip resistance compliance, lead times for imported materials, and protection of finished floors from damage by follow-on trades conducting final-fix work.

Ceramic and porcelain tiles are the most common floor finish in wet areas, lobbies, and common areas. Imported tiles from Italy, Spain, and China carry lead times of 8-14 weeks (with potential for 2-4 weeks additional delay during peak shipping periods), while domestic supply is 2-6 weeks. Imported premium tiles cost $60-$200/m2 for supply, standard domestic tiles $20-$60/m2, and installation costs $40-$120/m2 depending on tile size, pattern, and substrate condition. Order quantities must include 10% wastage plus 5% attic stock for future maintenance and repairs — failing to order attic stock at the time of the main order means that replacement tiles from a different production batch may not match in colour and shade.

Large-format tiles (600x1200mm and above) require specialist installation with lippage control systems (levelling clips and wedges) and must be priced and programmed accordingly — they require more skilled labour and slower installation rates than standard-format tiles. Substrate flatness tolerance for large-format tiles is 3mm in 3m (AS 3958.1), which is tighter than for standard tiles and frequently requires additional levelling compound, adding 1-2 days per area.

Slip resistance is a critical compliance requirement governed by AS 4586-2013 (Slip resistance classification of new pedestrian surface materials). NCC 2024 requires specific slip resistance classifications for different areas — typically R10 for internal dry areas, R11 for wet areas, P3-P5 for ramps and stairs. Slip resistance must be verified before ordering tiles, not after — a tile that does not meet the required classification cannot be used, regardless of aesthetic preference. Slip resistance testing to AS 4586 is conducted by accredited laboratories and takes 2-4 weeks for results. Some tile suppliers provide slip resistance certificates with their product data, but the PM should verify that the certificate is current and that the testing was conducted to AS 4586.

Timber flooring (solid and engineered) is governed by the Timber Flooring Association (TFA) guidelines and requires substrate moisture testing before installation. Concrete substrates must have a moisture content below 75% relative humidity (ASTM F2170 in-situ probe test) or 5.5% (CM method) before timber flooring can be installed — higher moisture levels cause timber to swell, cup, and buckle after installation. Moisture testing takes 72 hours for in-situ probe tests and should be programmed 2-3 weeks before planned timber flooring installation to allow for remediation (dehumidification or moisture barrier application) if results are out of specification.

Reference: AS 3958.1-2007 (ceramic tiles — guide to installation); AS 4586-2013 (slip resistance); NCC 2024 Part D (access and egress — slip resistance); TFA Installation Guidelines; ASTM F2170 (concrete moisture testing)
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes
Related standards: AS 1884-2021 (floor coverings — resilient sheet and tiles); AS 3740-2021 (waterproofing — substrate interface)

Common pitfalls:
- Not ordering 10% wastage plus 5% attic stock with the main tile order — replacement tiles from a different batch will not colour-match, and the original batch may be discontinued
- Failing to verify slip resistance classification to AS 4586 before ordering tiles — discovering non-compliance after delivery results in re-procurement and programme delay
- Installing timber flooring over a concrete substrate with moisture content above specification — the timber will swell, cup, and buckle within 6-12 months, requiring full replacement ($80-$200/m2)
- Not protecting finished floors from damage by follow-on trades (MEP final fix, painting touch-up, furniture delivery) — floor damage during this period is the most common post-handover defect claim

## Joinery and Cabinetry — Shop Drawing Process, Site Measure, and Installation Sequencing

Joinery is typically the last major trade installation before painting and handover, which means any delay to joinery cascades directly to practical completion. Domestic joinery fabrication carries a lead time of 8-14 weeks (1 week site measure, 2-3 weeks shop drawings, 1-2 weeks approval, 4-8 weeks fabrication), while imported joinery extends to 14-22 weeks (2-4 weeks design, 8-12 weeks fabrication, 4-6 weeks shipping). Residential joinery costs $15,000-$60,000 per dwelling, and commercial joinery costs $200-$800/m2 of joinery face. These figures make joinery one of the highest-value architectural trade packages on a per-unit basis.

The cardinal rule of joinery procurement is: never fabricate from drawing dimensions alone. Site-measure is non-negotiable. The joinery subcontractor must attend site after walls are lined and set (but before painting), take actual field measurements, and produce shop drawings based on those measurements. Drawing dimensions assume perfect construction, which does not exist — walls are not perfectly plumb, corners are not perfectly square, and floor-to-ceiling heights vary by 5-20mm across a typical apartment. Fabricating joinery from drawing dimensions results in gaps, misalignment, and field modifications that compromise quality and increase installation time. For repetitive layouts (apartment projects), the PM should require a first-article (prototype) unit to be fabricated, installed, and approved before full production commences. The prototype identifies dimensional, material, and finish issues that can be corrected before hundreds of identical units are fabricated.

Imported joinery offers 30-50% cost savings over domestic fabrication for equivalent specification, but carries significantly higher programme risk. The lead time extends to 14-22 weeks, and there is no local rework capacity — dimensional errors require either return-to-factory (adding 8-16 weeks and shipping costs) or field modification (expensive, with inevitable quality compromise). The PM must invest heavily in design coordination and prototyping for imported joinery, and must include contingency for shipping delays (sea freight for joinery is the only viable option — air freight is prohibitively expensive).

The engineered stone crisis is reshaping joinery procurement. SafeWork Australia has issued guidance on the prohibition of uncontrolled dry cutting of engineered stone, and several states (NSW, VIC, QLD) have implemented or announced bans on engineered stone benchtop fabrication without strict dust controls due to the silicosis risk. Some jurisdictions are moving towards complete bans. PMs must verify that their stone supplier and fabricator comply with all current WHS requirements, and should consider alternative benchtop materials (natural stone, porcelain slab, solid surface) where engineered stone compliance is uncertain. Engineered stone benchtops cost $400-$1,200 per linear metre installed.

Coordination of services behind joinery is critical. Power outlets, data points, water supply, and waste connections that are concealed behind or within joinery must be installed and tested before the site measure takes place. If services are relocated or added after joinery is fabricated, the joinery must be modified on site — at significant cost and quality loss.

Reference: NATSPEC joinery specifications; SafeWork Australia engineered stone guidance; AS 4386-1996 (domestic kitchen installations); NCC 2024 Part G (amenity provisions)
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — residential and commercial
Related standards: WHS Act and Regulations (engineered stone); AS 1428.1 (accessible kitchen requirements)

Common pitfalls:
- Fabricating joinery from drawing dimensions without site-measure — walls are never perfectly plumb or square, resulting in gaps, misalignment, and costly field modifications
- Not requiring a first-article (prototype) unit for repetitive apartment layouts before full production, allowing dimensional and finish errors to be replicated across hundreds of units
- Procuring imported joinery without investing in design coordination, prototyping, and factory quality inspection — dimensional errors discovered on site have no local rework solution
- Not coordinating services behind joinery (power, data, water, waste) before site-measure, resulting in post-fabrication modifications that compromise quality and programme

## Painting and Protective Coatings — Surface Preparation, Application, and Intumescent Systems

Painting is the final wet trade in the internal finishes sequence and requires all preceding trades — linings, joinery, doors, hardware — to be substantially complete before it can commence. Concurrent works in painting areas are the primary cause of painting defects, because dust and debris from other trades contaminate wet paint surfaces, requiring sanding, re-preparation, and recoating. The PM must enforce a clear rule: no concurrent works in active painting zones.

Standard internal painting costs are low in material terms ($3-$8/m2) but labour-intensive ($12-$30/m2 for a three-coat system). A standard three-coat system comprises one coat of primer/sealer and two coats of finish paint. For premium applications requiring Level 4 or Level 5 finish quality, the plasterboard must receive a minimum 2mm skim coat before painting — this is a lining trade responsibility but directly affects paint quality and must be specified at the lining stage, not discovered at the painting stage.

Painter production rates are typically 25-40m2 per painter per day for walls and lower for ceilings (restricted by overhead working position, cutting-in at perimeters, and access requirements). These rates assume a clear workspace — concurrent works can reduce productivity by 30-50% due to access conflicts, dust contamination requiring re-work, and stop-start disruption. The PM should programme painting as an exclusive-possession activity: once painting starts in an area, no other trade enters until painting is complete.

Intumescent and specialist protective coatings are a fundamentally different proposition from standard painting. Intumescent coatings are applied to structural steel to achieve fire-resistance levels (FRL) — the coating expands (intumesces) when exposed to fire, forming an insulating char that protects the steel from heat. Intumescent coating costs $80-$200/m2, compared to $15-$40/m2 for standard external paint. The coating must be applied by a certified applicator to a specific dry-film thickness (DFT), with independent DFT testing required at each coat stage. If the coating fails DFT testing, additional coats are required, adding cost and programme time. Application is weather-sensitive (above 10 degrees Celsius, below 35 degrees Celsius, below 85% relative humidity), and the completed coating must be protected from mechanical damage by subsequent trades.

Material supply for intumescent coatings carries a 4-8 week lead time, and the applicator must be certified by the coating manufacturer. The PM must programme intumescent coating application before steel is enclosed by linings, facade elements, or ceilings — once the steel is enclosed, access for coating application or remediation is extremely expensive (requiring removal and reinstatement of other elements).

Touch-up and clean is the final painting activity and should be programmed as a discrete 1-2 week activity before practical completion inspection. Touch-up addresses damage caused by follow-on trades (MEP final fix, floor finish installation, furniture delivery) and is inevitable on every project. Allowing inadequate programme time for touch-up is a common cause of projects failing practical completion inspection on aesthetic grounds.

Reference: AS 2311-2009 (painting of buildings); AS/NZS 2312 (corrosion protection of structural steel); NCC 2024 Part C (fire — intumescent coatings on structural steel); NATSPEC painting specifications
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes
Related standards: APAS (Australian Paint Approval Scheme) certification; NACE/SSPC surface preparation standards

Common pitfalls:
- Allowing concurrent works in painting areas, causing dust contamination of wet paint surfaces — rework costs 30-50% of original painting cost per affected area
- Not specifying Level 4/5 skim coat at the lining stage for premium applications, discovering the deficiency only when paint finish quality is visibly inadequate
- Failing to programme intumescent coating application before steel enclosure — retrofitting intumescent coatings after linings are installed requires demolition and reinstatement at 3-5x the original cost
- Allocating insufficient programme time for touch-up and clean before practical completion inspection — 1-2 weeks is the minimum for any project

## Door Hardware and Ironmongery — Keying Schedules, Fire Compliance, and Access Control

Door hardware procurement is deceptively complex. The hardware schedule — specifying the hinge type, lock function, handle style, closer specification, and any electronic access control for every door on the project — must be finalised early, ideally before door fabrication commences, because hardware selection determines door preparation (hinge mortises, lock mortise dimensions, closer arm mounting). Changing hardware after doors are fabricated requires field modification or door replacement.

Standard commercial hardware costs $800-$2,500 per door, premium architectural hardware $2,000-$8,000 per door, and electronic access control hardware $1,500-$5,000 per door. Lead times are 6-12 weeks for standard hardware and 12-20 weeks for specialist, premium, or electronic access control hardware including programming. The total hardware package on a 200-door commercial project can easily exceed $500,000, yet hardware is frequently treated as an afterthought, with specification decisions deferred until late in the construction programme.

Fire door hardware must comply with AS 1905.1 (Components for the protection of openings in fire-resistant walls). This is a compliance-critical requirement — a fire door fitted with non-compliant hardware (wrong closer, incorrect latch, missing smoke seal) is a non-compliant fire door, regardless of the door leaf's fire rating. AS 1905.1 specifies requirements for door closers (force, latching, closing speed), hinges (number, material, fire-tested), locks and latches (fire-tested to AS 1530.4), and smoke and draught seals. The PM must ensure that the fire door hardware schedule is reviewed by the fire engineer and that every fire door is fitted with hardware that matches the tested configuration documented in the fire door manufacturer's certificate.

Master keying schedules require coordination with the client or end-user and typically take 4-6 weeks for sign-off. The master keying schedule defines which keys open which doors, the hierarchy of master keys, grand master keys, and sub-master keys, and any areas with restricted or registered key profiles. This must be coordinated with electronic access control zones where electronic hardware is also installed — a common coordination failure is specifying a mechanical master-key system for doors that also have electronic access control, resulting in two independent access systems that confuse building management.

Electronic access control hardware is evolving rapidly, with wireless locks, mobile credential systems, and cloud-managed platforms increasingly replacing traditional hardwired card-reader systems. Technology selection should involve the security consultant early in the project, and the PM should verify that the selected system integrates with the building's broader security infrastructure (CCTV, intercoms, lift control, alarm monitoring). Lead times for electronic access control hardware should be verified at procurement stage as this market is highly dynamic.

Reference: AS 1905.1-2015 (fire door components); NCC 2024 Part C (fire doors); AS 4145 series (locksets); AS 4702 (fire door closers)
Applies to: new, refurb — commercial and multi-residential
Related standards: AS 1428.1 (accessible door hardware — lever handles, accessible lock heights); BCA/NCC fire door compliance schedules

Common pitfalls:
- Deferring hardware specification until after doors are fabricated, requiring field modification of door preparations — $200-$500 per door in rework costs
- Installing non-compliant hardware on fire doors — wrong closer, missing smoke seal, or untested lock — creating a compliance failure that may not be detected until fire safety inspection
- Not allowing 4-6 weeks for master keying schedule sign-off by the client, creating a bottleneck that delays lock cylinder ordering and door installation
- Failing to coordinate mechanical master-keying with electronic access control zones, resulting in two independent access systems with conflicting security logic

## Roofing Systems — Metal Deck, Membrane Roofing, and Weathertightness

Roof completion is the primary weathertightness milestone for single-storey buildings and the upper levels of multi-storey buildings. Until the roof is complete and weathertight, no internal finishes can commence in the areas below. A roofing delay of one week on a single-storey building delays handover by approximately one week; on a multi-storey building, roofing delays compound with facade delays to extend the weathertightness milestone.

Metal roofing (Colorbond and Zincalume profiles by BlueScope) is the dominant roofing material in Australia for commercial, industrial, and residential construction. Standard profiles carry a lead time of 4-8 weeks, with custom flashings adding 2-4 weeks. Installed costs range from $80-$200/m2 including insulation and sarking. Custom colours beyond the standard Colorbond range add 6-10 weeks to lead time. Standing-seam metal roofing systems, which provide a concealed-fix, flat-profile finish for architectural applications, carry an 8-12 week fabrication lead time and a cost premium of 30-50% over standard profiles.

Metal roofing must comply with AS 1562.1 (Design and installation of sheet roof and wall cladding — Metal). Key requirements include minimum roof pitch (1 degree for longrun profiles with appropriate sealing, 5 degrees for standard profiles without sealant at laps), fastener type and spacing, end-lap and side-lap requirements, and provision for thermal movement (metal roofing expands and contracts significantly with temperature changes — a 30m roof sheet can move 15-20mm between winter and summer). Failure to accommodate thermal movement results in fastener pull-out, sheet buckling, and water ingress at laps.

Box gutter design is a critical PM focus area. Box gutters are internal gutters concealed within the roof structure, and their failure (blockage or overflow) causes internal flooding that damages ceilings, walls, equipment, and stock. AS/NZS 3500.3 (Plumbing and drainage — Stormwater drainage) governs box gutter sizing, and NCC 2024 requires box gutters to be designed for 1-in-100 year average recurrence interval (ARI) storm events with an overflow provision. The PM must verify that box gutter design includes adequate falls (minimum 1:200, preferably 1:100), overflow provisions (either overflow outlets or freeboard to adjacent roof sheeting level), and maintenance access for cleaning.

Membrane roofing (for flat or near-flat roofs where metal sheeting is not appropriate) uses the same membrane types as external waterproofing — torch-on modified bitumen, TPO, PVC, or liquid-applied membranes. Lead times, costs, and application constraints are similar to those described in the External Waterproofing section. The critical additional requirement for membrane roofs is falls — NCC 2024 requires minimum 1:100 falls to drainage points on flat roofs, and ponding water on membrane roofs accelerates UV degradation and increases the risk of membrane failure.

Reference: AS 1562.1-2018 (metal roof and wall cladding); AS/NZS 3500.3-2021 (stormwater drainage — box gutter design); NCC 2024 Part F1 (damp and weatherproofing); BlueScope technical documentation
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes
Related standards: AS 4654.1/4654.2 (external waterproofing — for membrane roofs); AS 1170.2 (wind actions — for roof cladding design)

Common pitfalls:
- Not accommodating thermal movement in long-run metal roofing, resulting in fastener pull-out and sheet buckling — a 30m sheet moves 15-20mm seasonally
- Undersizing box gutters or omitting overflow provisions, causing internal flooding during high-intensity storm events — remediation of internal water damage can exceed $100,000 per event
- Specifying custom Colorbond colours without accounting for the 6-10 week additional lead time, discovering the delay after standard-colour production slots have been allocated
- Failing to achieve minimum falls on flat membrane roofs, resulting in ponding water that accelerates UV degradation and membrane failure within 5-10 years (design life should be 15-25 years)

## Fire-Rated Construction — Compartmentation, Penetration Sealing, and Compliance

Fire-rated construction is the second highest hidden risk in Australian building projects, behind waterproofing. Non-compliance with fire compartmentation requirements is often invisible — sealed inside walls, above ceilings, and within service risers — and may only be discovered during fire safety audits, insurance inspections, or actual fire events. Post-Grenfell regulatory scrutiny has significantly increased enforcement activity by state building authorities, and fire safety compliance audits of completed buildings are becoming more common.

Fire compartmentation divides a building into fire-rated compartments that contain fire and smoke spread for the time specified by the fire-resistance level (FRL). NCC 2024 Volume 1 Part C specifies the FRL requirements for different building elements — fire walls, fire-rated floors, fire-rated shafts, fire doors, and fire-rated ceiling/floor assemblies. The most common FRLs in commercial and multi-residential construction are 60/60/60 (1 hour), 90/90/90 (1.5 hours), and 120/120/120 (2 hours). Each fire compartment boundary — every wall, floor, door, and service penetration — must achieve or exceed the specified FRL. A single non-compliant element in a fire compartment boundary compromises the entire compartment.

Penetration sealing is the most common fire-rated construction failure mode. Every pipe, cable, duct, and conduit that passes through a fire-rated wall or floor creates a penetration that must be sealed with a fire-rated penetration sealing system tested to AS 1530.4 and AS 4072.1. The penetration seal must match the FRL of the element it penetrates. Common failures include: unsealed penetrations (left open by trades who drilled through the wall or floor), penetrations sealed with non-fire-rated materials (expanding foam, silicone, plaster — none of which achieve a fire rating), and penetrations sealed with a fire-rated product but not in accordance with the tested configuration (wrong annular gap, wrong seal depth, wrong backing material).

The PM must establish a penetration sealing regime from the start of construction. This includes: (1) a penetration register that tracks every penetration through every fire-rated element; (2) a hold-point inspection before fire-rated walls are lined (second side) and before ceilings are installed, to verify that all penetrations are sealed; (3) photographic documentation of every penetration seal (before and after sealing); and (4) a certificate from the penetration sealing subcontractor confirming that all seals comply with the tested system configuration. This documentation is required for the building's essential safety measures (ESM) compliance schedule and annual fire safety certification.

Fire-rated construction also includes fire collars on plastic pipes (which melt in fire, creating an opening through the fire-rated element — the fire collar expands to close the opening), fire dampers in ductwork that passes through fire-rated elements, and fire-stopping at the junction between fire-rated walls and the structural slab (head-of-wall fire-stopping). Each of these elements must be installed, inspected, and documented.

Reference: NCC 2024 Volume 1 Part C (fire resistance and stability); AS 1530.4-2014 (fire resistance testing); AS 4072.1-2005 (fire resistance of service penetrations and control joints); AS 1905.1 (fire doors)
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all Class 2-9 buildings
Related standards: AS 1668.1 (fire and smoke control in buildings); state fire safety legislation (Environmental Planning and Assessment Regulation in NSW, Building Regulations in VIC, Building Fire Safety Regulation in QLD)

Common pitfalls:
- Unsealed or incorrectly sealed penetrations through fire-rated elements — the single most common fire compliance failure, often discovered only during annual fire safety certification audits
- Trades drilling through fire-rated walls and floors for services without notifying the PM or the penetration sealing subcontractor, creating unsealed openings in fire compartment boundaries
- Using non-fire-rated sealants (expanding foam, silicone, general-purpose plaster) to seal penetrations through fire-rated elements — these materials do not achieve a fire rating and are a compliance failure
- Not documenting penetration seals with photographs and certificates, leaving the building without the documentation required for the essential safety measures compliance schedule

## Acoustic Performance and Compliance — Testing, Flanking, and Remediation

Acoustic defects are the fastest-growing complaint category in multi-residential construction, driven by NCC 2024 tightening of acoustic separation requirements and increasing occupant expectations for acoustic privacy. Acoustic defects are among the most expensive to remediate because rectification typically requires strip-out of completed wall and floor finishes, demolition of linings, upgrading of acoustic insulation and mounting systems, and reinstatement of all finishes — a process that is disruptive, costly ($10,000-$30,000+ per defective wall or floor assembly), and often only partially effective if the root cause is structural flanking rather than direct sound transmission.

NCC 2024 Part F5 requires an impact-weighted sound reduction index (Rw + Ctr) of at least 50 for walls separating sole-occupancy units (apartments) in Class 2 buildings, and similar requirements for floors. This represents a step-change from earlier editions and has driven widespread adoption of resilient-mounted lining systems, acoustic underlays for floor finishes, and more careful detailing of wall-floor junctions.

The critical distinction for PMs is between direct sound transmission (through the partition or floor itself) and flanking sound transmission (around, over, or under the partition via connected building elements). A partition that achieves Rw 55 in laboratory testing may only achieve Rw 45-48 in situ if flanking paths are not controlled. The most common flanking paths in multi-residential construction are: (1) through the ceiling plenum — sound travels over the top of the partition through the shared ceiling void, requiring a fire/acoustic barrier above the partition line extending to the underside of the slab above; (2) through the floor — sound travels under the partition through a continuous floor slab or screed, requiring an acoustic break at the partition line; (3) through back-to-back services — electrical outlets, plumbing penetrations, and HVAC grilles placed back-to-back on an inter-tenancy wall create direct sound paths through the wall; (4) through window mullion connections — sound travels along the facade mullion system from one apartment to the adjacent apartment, requiring acoustic isolation at the mullion-partition junction.

Pre-completion acoustic testing should be specified in the contract as a compliance verification measure. Testing to AS/NZS ISO 140 series (field measurement of airborne and impact sound insulation) is conducted by an accredited acoustic consultant and costs $500-$1,500 per test location. The PM should programme testing early enough (typically 4-6 weeks before practical completion) to allow for remediation of any failures before handover. Testing after linings are complete but before painting is ideal — if failures are detected, remediation requires lining removal but not paint repair.

Reference: NCC 2024 Part F5 (sound insulation); AS/NZS ISO 140 series (acoustic testing); AS/NZS 1276 series (acoustics — rating of sound insulation); AAAC (Association of Australasian Acoustical Consultants) guidelines
Applies to: new, refurb — Class 2 (apartments), Class 3 (hotels/accommodation), Class 9c (aged care)
Related standards: NCC 2024 Part F5 Verification Method FV5; AWCI acoustic system specifications

Common pitfalls:
- Ignoring flanking sound transmission paths — a laboratory-rated Rw 55 partition that flanks through the ceiling plenum, floor, or back-to-back services will fail in-situ testing at Rw 45-48
- Not specifying pre-completion acoustic testing in the contract, leaving compliance unverified until occupants complain post-handover — remediation at that point costs 3-5x more due to occupied building disruption
- Placing back-to-back electrical outlets or plumbing penetrations on inter-tenancy walls, creating direct sound transmission paths that defeat the partition's acoustic rating
- Failing to programme acoustic testing 4-6 weeks before practical completion, leaving insufficient time for remediation if failures are detected

## Procurement and Tender Evaluation for Architectural Trades

Architectural trade packages collectively represent 35-50% of total construction cost on a typical commercial or multi-residential project. The procurement strategy for these packages — how they are structured, when they are tendered, how tenders are evaluated, and how long-lead items are managed — has a direct impact on project cost, programme, and quality outcomes. A PM who treats architectural trade procurement as a routine administrative exercise rather than a strategic programme management activity will lose programme time, overpay for materials, and accept quality risk.

Long-lead item identification and early procurement is the single most impactful procurement action the PM can take. The following items have lead times that place them on or near the critical programme path: unitised curtain wall (20-30 weeks), stick curtain wall (14-20 weeks), aluminium windows (10-16 weeks), natural stone (10-18 weeks), imported joinery (14-22 weeks), imported tiles (8-14 weeks), glass balustrades (8-14 weeks), specialist ceiling systems (8-14 weeks), specialist door hardware (12-20 weeks), and prefabricated bathroom pods (16-24 weeks). These items must be identified during pre-construction, their procurement timelines mapped against the construction programme, and early procurement (design-assist, early contractor involvement, or early supply agreements) initiated where the standard tender-award-fabrication sequence does not fit within the programme.

Tender evaluation for architectural trades must go beyond price. The evaluation criteria should include: (1) price (typically 40-50% weighting); (2) programme capability — can the tenderer meet the required lead times and installation rates? (3) quality track record — references from comparable recent projects; (4) financial capacity — can the tenderer sustain the cashflow requirements of the package? (5) technical compliance — does the tender meet the specified products, systems, and standards? (6) subcontractor workforce — does the tenderer have sufficient skilled labour, or is the price predicated on subcontracting to unknown third parties?

Package structuring decisions affect competition, coordination, and risk. Common architectural trade packages include: facade (curtain wall, windows, cladding — often a single package for coordination); waterproofing (external and internal, sometimes separated); plasterboard and ceilings (often combined as a single linings package); tiling and stone (often combined); joinery (kitchen, bathroom, general — may be separated if imported and domestic sources are used); painting (internal and external, usually combined); and hardware (often procured separately from doors). The PM should structure packages to maximise coordination efficiency — combining trades that have significant interfaces into a single package reduces the number of coordination interfaces the PM must manage.

Reference: AS 4120-1994 (Code of Tendering); AIQS procurement guidelines; NATSPEC trade specification framework
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all project types and scales
Related standards: AS 4122-2010 (engagement of consultants — for specialist trade consultants such as facade engineers, acoustic consultants)

Common pitfalls:
- Not identifying long-lead items during pre-construction and mapping their procurement timelines against the construction programme — discovering that curtain wall has a 20-30 week lead time after the construction contract is signed
- Evaluating architectural trade tenders on price alone, without assessing programme capability, quality track record, and financial capacity — the cheapest subcontractor is often the most expensive in total project cost
- Structuring packages that separate trades with significant interfaces (for example, separating windows from facade cladding), creating coordination gaps that the PM must manage at the daily level
- Not locking long-lead material prices at tender stage when markets are volatile — aluminium, glass, and imported stone prices can move 10-15% in the time between tender and procurement

## Sequence of Internal Finishes — Critical Dependencies PMs Must Manage Daily

The sequence of internal finishes is the most programme-critical knowledge a PM must command on any multi-storey project. Every step in the sequence is dependent on the prior step's completion and inspection approval. A disruption at any point in the chain cascades through every subsequent trade, and the cascade multiplies as it moves downstream because later trades have less programme float and higher daily costs.

The canonical 17-step internal finishes sequence is: (1) structure complete (slab, columns, shear walls); (2) external facade and windows installed to achieve WEATHERTIGHT milestone; (3) fire-rated shaft walls and inter-tenancy walls framed with one side lined; (4) MEP first-fix rough-in (pipes, ducts, cables, conduit within walls and ceiling zones); (5) waterproofing of wet areas with HOLD POINT for inspection and flood test; (6) fire-rated wall second-side lining with HOLD POINT for inspection before close-up; (7) ceiling grid installation after above-ceiling services are tested and inspected; (8) wall tiling in wet areas over approved waterproofing; (9) floor screeding and levelling if required; (10) joinery and cabinetry installation; (11) door hanging and hardware installation; (12) painting (three-coat system after all trades complete); (13) floor finishes — carpet, vinyl, timber as the LAST trade to protect from damage; (14) MEP final-fix (GPOs, switches, diffusers, sprinkler heads through ceiling); (15) painting touch-up and clean; (16) defect inspection and rectification; (17) practical completion.

The four critical interfaces where PMs most commonly lose programme weeks are: Step 2 to 3 (facade to internal framing) — every week of facade delay translates to approximately 0.8 weeks of handover delay, with limited overlap possible; Step 4 to 5 to 6 (MEP rough-in to waterproofing to lining close-up) — MEP rough-in must be 100% complete in wet area walls before waterproofing, and any late services change punches through completed waterproofing at $2,000-$5,000 per bathroom to re-waterproof; Step 5 hold point (flood testing) — flood testing takes 24-48 hours per bathroom, and 200 bathrooms at 10 per day requires 4 weeks of testing programme; and Steps 10 to 11 to 12 (joinery to doors to painting) — joinery and doors must be installed before painting, so late joinery means late painting means late handover.

The PM must manage this sequence through a combination of look-ahead programming (6-week rolling look-ahead at minimum), daily coordination with trade foremen, enforcement of hold points, and aggressive management of the facade-to-weathertight milestone. Zone-by-zone or floor-by-floor sequencing allows multiple steps to proceed simultaneously in different zones, but each zone must follow the full 17-step sequence in order. The number of concurrent work zones (and therefore the overall programme duration) is constrained by the available labour force for each trade, particularly for bottleneck trades such as waterproofing, tiling, and painting.

Reference: NATSPEC construction sequence guidelines; AIQS programme management guidance; project-specific construction programme (baseline and current revision)
Applies to: new, refurb — multi-storey commercial and residential
Related standards: AS 4000-1997 clause 33 (construction programme); AS 4902-2000 clause 33

Common pitfalls:
- Not programming flood testing as a discrete line item with adequate duration — 200 bathrooms at 10 per day = 4 weeks minimum, which must be sequenced with waterproofing and tiling crews
- Allowing MEP rough-in to continue in areas where waterproofing has commenced, resulting in membrane penetrations that require re-waterproofing at $2,000-$5,000 per bathroom
- Failing to enforce exclusive possession for painting zones, allowing concurrent works that contaminate wet paint and require 30-50% rework
- Underestimating the cascade effect of facade delays on internal finishes — a 4-week facade delay does not mean a 4-week handover delay, it means approximately 3.2 weeks of handover delay plus disruption costs across all internal trades

## Balustrade and Handrail Systems — Engineering Certification, Glass, and Safety Compliance

Balustrade and handrail systems are safety-critical elements governed by NCC 2024 Part D (Access and egress), specifically clauses D3.2 and D3.3 which specify barrier height requirements, loading, and openings restrictions. A non-compliant balustrade is a life-safety risk and a regulatory enforcement issue — state building authorities actively audit balustrade compliance, and non-compliant balustrades are among the most common reasons for withholding of occupation certificates.

Glass balustrade systems are the dominant architectural choice for balconies, terraces, stairways, and internal voids in modern commercial and multi-residential construction. They are available in three configurations: frameless (glass panels fixed to a channel or point-fixed to the structure), semi-frameless (glass panels in a bottom channel with exposed top edge), and framed (glass panels within an aluminium or steel frame). Costs range from $300-$700/lm for framed glass balustrades, $400-$900/lm for semi-frameless, and $600-$1,500/lm for frameless. Lead times are 8-14 weeks (2-3 weeks design, 2-3 weeks engineering certification, 4-8 weeks fabrication).

Engineering certification is a critical procurement milestone for glass balustrades. Every glass balustrade installation requires structural engineering certification confirming that: (1) the glass type and thickness are adequate for the design wind load and barrier loading per AS 1170.1 (dead and live loads) and AS 1170.2 (wind actions); (2) the fixing system (channel, spigots, or point fixings) is adequate for the applied loads; and (3) the structure to which the balustrade is fixed (slab edge, upstand beam, steel post) has adequate capacity to resist the balustrade loads without unacceptable deflection. This certification can take 2-3 weeks to obtain and can delay procurement if structural adequacy of the fixing is questioned — for example, if the slab edge is thinner than required for the proposed fixing system, or if the proposed fixings conflict with post-tensioning tendons.

Post-tensioned slab interfaces are a specific risk for glass balustrade base-plate fixings. Post-installed anchors drilled into post-tensioned slabs carry a risk of striking and damaging prestressing tendons, which can cause catastrophic structural failure. The PM must ensure that post-tensioned tendon locations are identified (by GPR scanning or reference to as-built PT shop drawings) before any balustrade base-plate anchors are drilled, and that the post-tensioning engineer provides written approval for the proposed anchor locations. This coordination step is frequently overlooked, with potentially devastating consequences.

All balustrade glass must be safety glass complying with AS 1288 — typically toughened (tempered) glass or laminated glass. NCC 2024 requires that barriers to all unenclosed edges of floors, mezzanines, balconies, and roof areas where the fall height exceeds 1 metre be at least 1000mm high (measured from the finished floor level), and in buildings where children under 5 may be present (Class 2 apartments, Class 3 accommodation, Class 9b schools), the barrier must be at least 1000mm high and must not have climbable elements or openings that allow passage of a 125mm sphere below 865mm from the floor.

Reference: NCC 2024 Part D3.2-D3.3 (barriers and handrails); AS 1170.1 (structural loads); AS 1170.2 (wind actions); AS 1288-2006 (glass in buildings); AS 1288 Amendment 1-2021
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes with balustrades and barriers
Related standards: AS 1657-2018 (fixed platforms, walkways, stairways, ladders — industrial handrails); AS 4687 (temporary fencing — for construction stage edge protection)

Common pitfalls:
- Not obtaining structural engineering certification before ordering glass balustrades, discovering structural inadequacy of the slab edge or fixing system after fabrication, requiring redesign and re-fabrication
- Drilling post-installed anchors into post-tensioned slabs without tendon location verification, risking tendon strike and catastrophic structural failure
- Fabricating balustrade glass from drawing dimensions rather than site-measured template — slab edges are never perfectly straight or level, resulting in glass panels that do not fit
- Specifying balustrade detailing that creates climbable elements or openings exceeding 125mm in buildings where children may be present, failing NCC D3.3 requirements

## Render and External Wall Finishes — Application, Movement Joints, and Weather Sensitivity

External render is the dominant external wall finish for residential construction in Australia (Class 1 houses and low-rise Class 2 apartments) and is also widely used on commercial buildings. Render systems are relatively low-cost ($60-$150/m2 for a three-coat acrylic render system, with texture coat adding $20-$40/m2) with short material lead times (2-4 weeks), but they are crack-prone, weather-sensitive during application, and require careful movement joint design to perform satisfactorily over the building's design life.

A standard three-coat acrylic render system comprises: (1) a base/scratch coat applied to the substrate (concrete, blockwork, or fibre-cement sheet on framing) with embedded fibreglass mesh at all change-of-substrate junctions and stress concentration points; (2) a levelling/body coat applied after the base coat has cured (minimum 7 days in normal conditions); and (3) a finish/texture coat applied after the body coat has cured. Total application time is 2-4 weeks per elevation, inclusive of cure time between coats. Application is weather-sensitive: render cannot be applied in rain, at temperatures below 10 degrees Celsius or above 35 degrees Celsius, or in direct sun on hot days (which causes premature surface drying and cracking). The PM must allow for weather holds in the render programme — in southern Australian winter, weather holds can consume 30-50% of the programmed render period.

Movement joints are the single most important detailing requirement for rendered external walls. Render is a rigid, brittle material that cannot accommodate the thermal and structural movements of the building substrate without cracking. AS 3700 (masonry) and render manufacturer specifications require control joints at maximum 6-8 metre centres for clay brick substrates and 4-6 metre centres for concrete blockwork substrates, and at all locations of stress concentration — adjacent to openings (windows and doors), at changes in wall height or thickness, at building movement joints, and at junctions between different substrates (for example, where a rendered blockwork wall meets a rendered timber-framed wall). Mesh reinforcement must be embedded across all change-of-substrate junctions to distribute cracking forces.

Missing or incorrectly placed movement joints are the primary cause of render cracking. Crack remediation costs $50-$150 per linear metre for cosmetic repair (crack filling and re-texturing) and significantly more if the cracking has allowed water ingress into the wall cavity, requiring strip-out and re-rendering of affected areas. The PM must review the render contractor's proposed movement joint layout before application commences and verify that joints are placed at all required locations.

Scaffold coordination is a practical programme consideration. External render is scaffold-dependent, and the scaffold must remain in place for the full duration of the render programme (including cure times between coats). If other trades (facade cladding, external waterproofing, window flashings) also require scaffold access, the PM must coordinate scaffold possession and access priorities to avoid scaffold standby costs ($2,000-$10,000 per week depending on scaffold length and height).

Reference: AS 3700-2018 (masonry structures — movement joint requirements); NCC 2024 Part F1 (weatherproofing); render manufacturer technical data sheets and system warranties; NATSPEC render specifications
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — residential and low-rise commercial
Related standards: AS 4773.1-2015 (masonry in small buildings — Part 1 design); AS 2311-2009 (painting — interface with rendered surfaces)

Common pitfalls:
- Omitting or incorrectly spacing movement joints, resulting in render cracking that requires remediation at $50-$150/lm — the most common render defect
- Not embedding fibreglass mesh reinforcement at change-of-substrate junctions (for example, where concrete meets timber framing), creating a crack line at the substrate boundary
- Programming render application without weather holds in winter, resulting in programme overrun when render cannot be applied for 30-50% of scheduled days due to cold, rain, or excessive heat
- Not coordinating scaffold possession with other trades, incurring scaffold standby costs of $2,000-$10,000 per week while waiting for other trades to complete their scaffold-dependent work

## Prefabricated Bathroom Pods and Modular Joinery — Procurement, Interfaces, and Quality Assurance

Prefabricated bathroom pods are factory-assembled, fully finished bathroom modules that are craned into the building structure and connected to services. They are gaining significant traction in repetitive building types — hotels, build-to-rent (BTR), student accommodation, and aged care — where the same bathroom layout is repeated dozens or hundreds of times. The key advantages are programme acceleration (pods can be manufactured off-site while the structure is being built, and installation takes hours rather than the weeks required for traditional bathroom construction), quality consistency (factory-controlled environment), and reduced on-site labour and waste. The key risks are the requirement for early design freeze, longer procurement lead times, structural interface coordination, and very limited capacity for site modification.

Prefabricated bathroom pod lead times are 16-24 weeks from design lock-in to first delivery (4-6 weeks design and engineering, 8-12 weeks fabrication, 4-6 weeks shipping for offshore-manufactured pods or 2-4 weeks transport for domestic manufacturers). This lead time means that the bathroom design must be finalised and locked during early construction — typically before the structure reaches level 3-4 on a multi-storey project. Any design change after the lock-in point (client requests for different fixtures, tile changes, layout modifications) requires a factory production change that resets the fabrication schedule and can add 4-8 weeks of delay.

The structural interface between the pod and the building structure is the primary coordination risk. Pods are typically manufactured with a structural steel or timber frame and are designed to sit on the structural slab. The slab must accommodate the pod's weight (typically 1.5-3.0 tonnes per pod), the pod's dimensional footprint (which must align with the structural grid), and the services connection points (waste, water supply, electrical, and ventilation connections at precise locations). The structural engineer must be involved in pod design coordination from the outset, and the PM must verify that slab penetrations and services stub-ups are installed to the pod manufacturer's precise tolerance requirements before pods are delivered. A misaligned waste connection or a slab penetration in the wrong location can prevent pod installation.

Cost is typically 5-15% higher than traditional bathroom construction for the pod itself, but total installed cost (including the programme savings, reduced on-site supervision, reduced defect rectification, and reduced waste disposal) is often comparable or lower on projects with more than 80-100 repetitive bathroom layouts. Below this threshold, the tooling and design costs are not amortised over enough units to justify the premium.

Quality assurance for prefabricated pods requires factory inspection before shipping. The PM or a third-party QA inspector should inspect a sample of completed pods at the factory to verify dimensional accuracy, waterproofing integrity (factory flood test), tile and finish quality, and services connection point locations. Dimensional errors or finish defects discovered after pods arrive on site have very limited remediation options — pods are too large to return to factory economically, and on-site modification compromises the factory quality that justified the pod approach.

Current Australian pod manufacturers include Interpod, Moduler, Hickory, and several new entrants. The market is maturing rapidly, and cost benchmarks and lead times are shifting as production capacity increases. The PM should focus on the procurement process and coordination requirements (which are stable) rather than relying on manufacturer-specific data that may change.

Reference: NATSPEC prefabrication specifications; NCC 2024 (applicable to pods as for any bathroom — AS 3740 waterproofing, NCC Part F amenity); manufacturer-specific technical documentation and installation guides
Applies to: new — hotels, BTR, student accommodation, aged care (Class 3, Class 9c), high-repetition Class 2
Related standards: AS 3740-2021 (waterproofing — applies to pod interiors); AS 3500 series (plumbing — services connections)

Common pitfalls:
- Not locking bathroom design early enough — any change after the pod design lock-in point resets the fabrication schedule by 4-8 weeks
- Failing to coordinate slab penetrations and services stub-ups to the pod manufacturer's tolerance requirements, resulting in pods that cannot be connected on site
- Not conducting factory QA inspection before pods are shipped, discovering dimensional or finish defects on site with very limited remediation options
- Underestimating the structural engineering coordination required — pod weight (1.5-3.0 tonnes), footprint alignment, and services connection locations all require early structural involvement

## Accessibility Compliance — DDA, AS 1428, and Premises Standards

Accessibility compliance is a cross-cutting requirement that affects multiple architectural trades — doors, wet areas, floor finishes, hardware, signage, and thresholds — yet it is frequently treated as an afterthought, resulting in expensive late-stage rectification. The Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (Cth), the Disability (Access to Premises — Buildings) Standards 2010 (Premises Standards), and AS 1428 series provide the regulatory and technical framework for accessible building design in Australia. NCC 2024 references the Premises Standards and AS 1428 extensively, and compliance is a condition of building certification.

The most common and costly accessibility compliance failures in construction are: (1) door clear opening widths — AS 1428.1 requires a minimum 850mm clear opening width for accessible doors (measured with the door open to 90 degrees), but standard 820mm door leaves in standard frames frequently fail to achieve 850mm clear width after hardware installation, requiring door and frame replacement at $1,500-$3,000 per door; (2) threshold heights at external doors and balcony doors — AS 1428.1 limits threshold heights to 5mm maximum for accessible paths of travel (or 20mm with a bevelled edge), which conflicts with waterproofing upstand requirements of 150mm minimum per AS 4654.2, requiring careful design of threshold drainage details that satisfy both standards; (3) accessible sanitary facilities — AS 1428.1 specifies precise dimensions for accessible toilets, showers, and basins (grab rail heights, fixture locations, circulation space, and door swing direction), and even small dimensional errors (a grab rail 20mm too low or a toilet pan 30mm too far from the wall) constitute non-compliance.

The PM's role in accessibility compliance is primarily coordination and inspection. During design, the PM should verify that the access consultant's requirements are integrated into the architectural, hydraulic, and electrical drawings — accessible fixtures, fittings, and finishes must be specified and dimensioned on the trade drawings, not only on the access consultant's diagrams. During construction, the PM should establish hold-point inspections for accessible facilities before linings are closed (to verify structural backing for grab rails), before waterproofing (to verify threshold and hob details), and before final fix (to verify fixture locations and heights).

Floor finish slip resistance is an accessibility interface. The Premises Standards require that accessible paths of travel have slip-resistant floor surfaces, and AS 1428.1 references AS 4586 for slip resistance classification. Tactile ground surface indicators (TGSIs) must be installed at the top and bottom of stairs, at pedestrian crossings, and at other hazard locations as specified by AS 1428.4 — these are frequently omitted or incorrectly located, and remediation requires demolition and reinstatement of the floor finish at the affected locations.

Luminance contrast is an often-overlooked accessibility requirement. AS 1428.1 requires a minimum 30% luminance contrast between critical building elements — door handles against doors, doors against door frames, stair nosings against treads, TGSIs against the surrounding floor, and sanitary fixtures against walls and floors. Luminance contrast is measured using a light reflectance value (LRV) meter, not by visual assessment. Material selections (paint colours, tile colours, fixture finishes) must be checked for luminance contrast compliance before procurement.

Reference: Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (Cth); Disability (Access to Premises — Buildings) Standards 2010; AS 1428.1-2021 (general requirements for access); AS 1428.2-1992 (enhanced and additional requirements); AS 1428.4-2009 (tactile indicators); AS 4586-2013 (slip resistance); NCC 2024 Part D (access and egress)
Applies to: new, refurb, extend — all building classes (with differing requirements by class)
Related standards: AS 1735.12 (accessible lifts); AS 2890.6 (accessible parking)

Common pitfalls:
- Specifying standard 820mm door leaves that do not achieve the 850mm clear opening width required by AS 1428.1 after hardware installation — requiring door and frame replacement at $1,500-$3,000 per door
- Not resolving the conflict between accessible threshold heights (5mm maximum per AS 1428.1) and waterproofing upstand requirements (150mm minimum per AS 4654.2) during design, discovering the conflict during construction
- Omitting or incorrectly locating tactile ground surface indicators (TGSIs), requiring demolition and reinstatement of finished floor surfaces
- Not verifying luminance contrast between adjacent building elements during material selection — a 30% contrast requirement cannot be assessed visually and requires LRV measurement

## Signage, Wayfinding, and Statutory Signage — Procurement Timing and Compliance

Signage is a frequently deferred procurement item that causes handover delays on a disproportionate number of projects relative to its cost. Statutory signage (exit signs, fire indicator panel signage, fire safety notices, accessible facility signage) has specific NCC compliance requirements that must be met before an occupation certificate can be issued. General wayfinding and tenant signage is project-specific but typically has lead times of 4-8 weeks for design, approval, fabrication, and installation — a timeline that requires the PM to initiate procurement no later than 8-10 weeks before practical completion.

Exit signage must comply with AS/NZS 2293 (Emergency escape lighting and exit signs). Exit signs must be illuminated (either internally illuminated or photoluminescent per AS/NZS 2293.3), located at every required exit per NCC 2024 Part E (fire safety — escape), and connected to the building's emergency lighting circuit with battery backup. The fire engineer's exit sign schedule should be cross-referenced with the electrical drawings to verify that every required exit sign location has a power supply and that the emergency lighting circuit serves all signs. Exit signs must be commissioned and tested (90-minute battery discharge test per AS/NZS 2293) before the building can achieve compliance.

Accessible signage must comply with AS 1428.1, which requires Braille and tactile signage at accessible sanitary facilities, lifts, accessible exits, and other locations specified by the access consultant. Braille and tactile signs have specific dimensional requirements (character height, Braille dot spacing, mounting height of 1200-1600mm above floor level on the latch side of doors) and must be fabricated by a specialist signage manufacturer. Lead times for Braille and tactile signage are typically 3-6 weeks from design approval.

Fire safety notices — including the building fire safety schedule, fire indicator panel instructions, and the annual fire safety statement display — must be installed before occupation. These are relatively straightforward items but are frequently forgotten until the pre-occupation inspection, at which point their absence can delay the occupation certificate.

The PM should maintain a signage checklist covering all statutory and general signage requirements, initiate procurement 8-10 weeks before practical completion, and include signage installation and commissioning in the pre-practical-completion inspection programme.

Reference: AS/NZS 2293 (emergency escape lighting and exit signs); NCC 2024 Part E (fire safety — escape); AS 1428.1 (accessible signage — Braille and tactile); Environmental Planning and Assessment Regulation (NSW) — fire safety schedules
Applies to: new, refurb — all building classes
Related standards: AS 1319-1994 (safety signs for the occupational environment); local council signage requirements

Common pitfalls:
- Deferring signage procurement until 2-4 weeks before practical completion, leaving insufficient time for the 4-8 week design-approval-fabrication-installation cycle
- Not cross-referencing the fire engineer's exit sign schedule with electrical drawings, resulting in exit sign locations without power supply that are discovered during commissioning
- Omitting Braille and tactile signage from the signage procurement package, discovering the requirement at the pre-occupation accessibility audit
- Not commissioning and testing exit sign battery backup (90-minute discharge test per AS/NZS 2293) before practical completion inspection, failing the essential safety measures compliance check
