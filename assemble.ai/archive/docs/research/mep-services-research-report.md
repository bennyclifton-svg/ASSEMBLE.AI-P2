## Pass 1 — Industry Landscape Survey

### Market Overview

The Australian MEP services sector represents approximately **35-45% of total construction cost** on a typical commercial or residential high-rise project. For a $100M commercial office tower, MEP packages typically total $35M-$45M, split roughly:
- Mechanical (HVAC): 12-18% of total project cost
- Electrical: 10-15% of total project cost
- Hydraulic (plumbing + fire): 8-12% of total project cost
- Fire protection (sprinklers, detection, hydrants): 4-6% of total project cost

### Current Market Conditions (2024-2026)

**Workforce:**
- Severe trades shortage across all MEP disciplines. NECA has reported a shortfall of **8,000-10,000 electrical workers** nationally [VERIFY current figure].
- Plumbing and mechanical trades similarly constrained — ABS data shows construction employment at near-record levels but demand outstripping supply.
- Immigration pipeline (482 visas for trades) is supplementing but not resolving shortages.
- Apprenticeship completion rates remain below 50% nationally for electrical trades.

**Supply Chain:**
- Post-COVID supply chain disruptions have partially normalized but lead times remain extended for:
  - **Switchboards:** Main switchboards 16-24 weeks (was 12-14 pre-COVID)
  - **Chillers:** 20-32 weeks depending on manufacturer (Daikin, Carrier, Trane, York)
  - **Transformers:** 26-52 weeks for pad-mount/kiosk transformers (global transformer shortage)
  - **AHUs:** 12-20 weeks for custom air handling units
  - **Fire sprinkler heads and valves:** 8-16 weeks (previously 4-8)
  - **Copper pipe and fittings:** Stable but price-volatile (tracking LME copper)
- Semiconductor shortage continues to impact BMS controllers, VSD drives, and smart metering equipment.

**Industry Shifts:**
1. **Electrification mandate:** Gas phase-out in Victoria (no new residential gas connections from Jan 2024), ACT already fully electric. Other states moving similarly. Major impact on hydraulic (no gas reticulation) and mechanical (heat pump adoption).
2. **EV charging infrastructure:** NCC 2022 introduced EV-readiness requirements. New commercial buildings require conduit and circuit provisions for future EV chargers. Electrical load calculations now routinely include EV allowance.
3. **Renewables integration:** Rooftop solar + battery storage increasingly standard on commercial projects. Impacts electrical design (inverter rooms, DC isolation, export limiting).
4. **Smart buildings / IoT:** BMS integration expanding beyond HVAC to include lighting (DALI-2), access control, energy monitoring, and predictive maintenance. Open protocols (BACnet, Modbus) vs proprietary systems remains a specification issue.
5. **Prefabrication / DfMA:** Growing uptake of prefabricated mechanical plant rooms, pre-wired electrical risers, and modular bathroom pods with pre-installed hydraulics. Shifts risk profile — more design effort upfront, fewer on-site coordination issues.
6. **NCC 2022 energy efficiency tightening:** Section J (Energy Efficiency) requirements significantly increased. Envelope + services energy budget approach. HVAC system efficiency requirements more stringent.
7. **Building commissioning formalisation:** Independent Commissioning Agents (ICA) now common on projects >$50M. CIBSE Commissioning Codes and ASHRAE Guideline 0 referenced in specifications.

|---|-------------|------------|---------------|-------------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **HVAC system not achieving design temperatures / humidity** | Mechanical | Undersized equipment; poor ductwork installation; inadequate commissioning; incorrect control sequences; building envelope underperformance allowing excess heat load | $50,000-$500,000+ depending on system size. ### Critical Program Interfaces (MEP-Specific)

| Interface | Risk | Mitigation |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| **Structural penetrations** | Core and slab penetrations must be cast into formwork. Missed penetrations = core drilling = structural assessment = delay | Penetration drawings issued to structural/formwork subcontractor 4 weeks before pour. Hold point sign-off. |
| **Ceiling zone coordination** | Architect allocates ceiling zone; MEP must fit within it. Typical commercial: 600-900mm. If structure is deeper than assumed, services are squeezed. | Coordination meeting at design stage to confirm ceiling zone depth. Consider 3D coordination at tender stage. |
| **Riser coordination** | Vertical risers carry all services between floors. Riser dimensions set by architect early. Changes are extremely expensive (structural modifications to riser shafts). | Riser sizing calculation at concept design stage; include 20% growth allowance; coordinate all 4 MEP disciplines in single riser layout drawing. |
| **Switchroom / plant room sizing** | Rooms sized during architectural design; equipment specified during detailed design. If equipment doesn't fit = redesign or structural modification. | Switchboard/plant equipment GA drawings approved before room is constructed. Include maintenance clearances per AS/NZS 3000 (switchboards) and manufacturer requirements (mechanical plant). |
| **Fire compartmentation** | Every penetration through a fire-rated wall/floor must be fire-stopped. Hundreds of penetrations per floor on a commercial building. | Fire stopping schedule; staged inspections; qualified fire stopping installer; photographic records of all penetrations before concealment. |
| **Roof plant coordination** | Rooftop mechanical plant (cooling towers, chillers, AHUs) requires structural support, crane access, acoustic treatment, and maintenance access. | Roof plant layout and structural requirements confirmed at DD stage. Crane access plan for installation and future replacement. |
| **Façade interface** | Louvres for ventilation (mechanical), cable entries (electrical), overflow provisions (hydraulic) all penetrate the building envelope. | Facade penetration schedule coordinated across all MEP disciplines and facade contractor. Weatherproofing details for each penetration. |
